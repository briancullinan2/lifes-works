Introduction

From the early 1930s to the later 1940s, Swing jazz dominated the American popular music scene and—Swing was dance music! Swing featured a great dance feel: moderate tempos, a ‘swing’ feeling that was very kinetic and active, popular songs arranged for big bands that were recognizable and an attraction in itself. Swing also benefited from the increasing sophistication of music technology (like the spread of radio and the development of electronic microphones and recording technology) and the reduction in price of means of acquiring music (record players, records, and radios all were dropping in price).

Swing was music that people purchased, consumed, and used for diversion. This was the time of the Great Depression through the Second World War. It was a time of terrific displacement. During the Depression, many African Americans and white Southerners moved to cities in the North, Midwest , and West looking for work. They took their music with them. Their Southern, rural music changed as they experience new, tougher conditions in the cities. Rural music and lyrics now became transformed into edgier, urban styles.

In jazz, music evolved into a more commercial style that crossed racial lines and appealed to majority and minority listeners. Swing was the popular music of its day. Swing is what you would have heard in clubs, on the radio, and hummed by almost everyone. Swing represents the high water mark for jazz’s popularity. Never before, nor since, has jazz been America’s main form of popular music like it was with Swing.
