New York Swing

Don Redman

After leading bands in the New York area for a few years, Henderson took a band into the popular Roseland Ballroom in 1924. Together with his arranger and alto saxophonist Don Redman, he created music that set the standard for jazz big bands from the late 1920’s on and became one of the most widely imitated.

Both Duke Ellington and Benny Goodman, whose bands would dominate the 1930s, were influenced by the Henderson Orchestra. Henderson hired the best jazz musicians available among them: Louis Armstrong (trumpet), Coleman Hawkins (tenor sax) and Rex Stewart (cornet).

Henderson and Redman took the trumpet, trombone, and clarinet front line of the earlier Dixieland ensemble and made each instrument the leader of four or five-person trumpet, trombone, and reed (clarinet and saxophone) section. With a three or four-piece rhythm section, the instrumentation of the big band was established by Henderson and is the standard today.

Redman, perhaps in cahoots with Henderson, devised a format for arranging that revolutionized the big band sound, while capturing the polyphonic (many solo lines sounded at the same time) New Orleans style of ensemble playing. It’s known as a ‘block passage’ or ‘block voicing.’

In a block passage or voicing the melody line is played by the lead instrument of a section. The other members of the section are each assigned a note of the chords that is used to harmonize the melody. The rhythm of all of the parts is the same as the melody, so all members of the section play the same rhythmic pattern.
