Conclusion

Cut 'n' mix and remix are important because they returned jazz to its popular music roots. Starting in the late 1940s with bebop, jazz increasingly rejected both mass audiences and popular music connections in an effort that saw it move toward art music. Cut 'n' mix and remix have reconnected some strains of jazz with very creative popular artists and, most directly, to younger listeners.

Early hip-hop and jazz fusion (cut 'n' mix) as found in the 1970s through the 1980s was characterized by straight-ahead hip-hop, vamp-driven grooves that feature scratches, synthesizers, drum machines, and a prominent bass. There may or may not berapping or voice over by a DJ. If present, often the DJ will be referencing the jazz artist who is a collaborator on the recording. A solo jazz improv line, if present, is frequently just layered into the mix.

1990s acid jazz features much more interaction between the groove and a jazz solo line, compared to 1980s cut 'n' mix. There is also a much more prominent use of recordings that is starting to move beyond the sampling of cut 'n' mix toward the large-scale refashioning of preexisting recordings that will become remix.

Remix differs from cut 'n' mix in that remix reshuffles, reproduces, tears apart and reassembles a complete preexisting recording, rather than cut 'n' mix's use of samples.
