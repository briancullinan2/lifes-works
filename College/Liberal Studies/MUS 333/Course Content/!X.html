Dixieland style

By the early twentieth century, the brass bands and orchestras of Storyville were all featuring the latest music played in the city’s own distinct style. Also called Dixieland by later musical marketers, the music had the following characteristics.

  • Dixieland’s tempo was moderate to moderately fast (remember that this was dance music) and very lively and exciting. Usually Dixieland pieces were in duple meter.
  • Dixieland is a quite distinct jazz style, in that the ensembles practiced group improvisation, not as individual soloists. It was very spontaneous and only limited by the chord progression. Musicians would at times try to outdo one another and at other times cooperated in an ensemble passage.
  • Dixieland used primarily three forms: 32-bar AABA song form, 12-bar instrumental blues, and four-strain Ragtime form.

 

 

Front line

Front line

The front line musicians were the melody makers such as cornet, clarinet, and trombone. The front line was made up of soloists who simultaneously improvised lines (a musical texture called 'polyphony' with no unison playing) that were full of syncopation, polyrhythms, cross rhythms, and call-and-response patterns. In general, the front line’s improvisation was very unpredictable.

Second line

Second line

The second line musicians are what will be called the rhythm section in later jazz (guitar or banjo, tuba, and drums). The second line played static rhythmic patters in unison. While the front line was unpredictable and highly syncopated, the second line was as much of a contrast as can be imagined: they were very predictable and non-syncopated.

Dixieland ensemble (conclusion)

Dixieland ensemble (conclusion)

So, the Dixieland ensemble balanced a wonderful contradiction within itself—the two extremes of predictability and the unpredictable; syncopation and non-syncopation; improvisation and static lines.

Dixieland ensemble

Dixieland ensemble

The typical Dixieland ensemble numbered eight players on average. They were divided by function into two sections: the ‘front line’ and ‘second line.’

 

New Orleans (1900-1917)

New Orleans (1900-1917)

New Orleans had historically been one of the major ports of the African slave trade. It was an international city, with strong French roots. By the early decades of the twentieth century, there were Italian, French, and English opera houses and a thriving African American musical community. The breadth of African American music in New Orleans was huge, from African American middle class musicians and musicians playing for the middle class—with its emphasis on European art music—to the many bands that attracted patrons for the bars, gambling houses, dancehalls, and houses of prostitution in poorer quarters. The latter places were concentrated in a thirty-eight square block area of New Orleans noted for its legalized gambling and prostitution. Called Storyville after the city councilman who established the red-light district, this was New Orleans ’ main tourist attraction as people steamed down the Mississippi to the city.

“Dippermouth Blues”

“Dippermouth Blues”

King Oliver’s Creole Jazz Band recorded “Dippermouth Blues” in 1923 in Richmond , Indian just outside of Chicago. The Creole Jazz Band was another all-star ensemble that featured incredible talent: both Oliver and Louis Armstrong on cornets, Honré Dutrey on trombone, Johnny Dodds on clarinet, Lil Hardin (soon to be Mrs. Armstrong) on piano, Bill Johnson on banjo and vocal break, and drummer Baby Dodds.

“Dippermouth Blues” is a 12 bar blues and features the dense, polyphonic style of Dixieland that was so characteristic of the style.

Joseph ‘King’ Oliver

Joseph ‘King’ Oliver

King Oliver was both the mentor of the young Louis Armstrong and one of the most important of all New Orleans Dixieland band leaders.

Read the article on Joseph ‘King’ Oliver

When King Oliver was at the peak of his popularity in 1918, he moved to Chicago. He eventually enticed Louis Armstrong to move north and join his band working and recording in the city. Most importantly, King Oliver’s group was the first African American ensemble to record a series of jazz pieces (forty-two sides in 1923 alone).

 

“Carolina Shout” (1)

“Carolina Shout” (1)

Johnson recorded “Carolina Shout” in 1921 and it is a classic example of New York stride piano playing at its best. The ‘shout’ in the title refers to a ringshout, or celebratory African American religious dance.

As said earlier, New Yorker stride developed from ragtime and “Carolina Shout” retains some Ragtime features, such as Ragtime’s oompah-like left hand (bass line) and Ragtime’s formal structure.

“Carolina Shout” (2)

“Carolina Shout” (2)

“Carolina Shout” goes way beyond Ragtime as Johnson uses what were called ‘crushed’ thirds (playing the third scale degree from both the major minor scale at the same time), mixing in triple subdivisions (a swing feel), and an intense syncopation between bass line and treble all coupled with an incredible rhythmic drive.

“Carolina Shout” four beats to the bar. The song is divided up more by harmonic (chords) than by melodic (melody) phrases. The form is A A B C C D B D.

James P. Johnson and stride piano

James P. Johnson and stride piano

As New Orleans style Dixieland move north to cities like Chicago and New York , there developed in New York an analogous style of piano playing that was rooted in earlier Ragtime rather than New Orleans ’ blues. James P. Johnson was one of the best of the New York stride pianists. Duke Ellington learned Johnson solos note-for-note as a young pianist listening to Johnson’s best-selling piano rolls.

Louis Armstrong and His Hot Five

Louis Armstrong and His Hot Five

We first heard Louis Armstrong and His Hot Five earlier in the ‘Dixieland to Swing’ section of the course. They were the hottest band on record and only appeared together in the studio. The front line most often consisted of the best in the business: Jimmy Dodds on clarinet, Kid Ory on trombone, and anchored by Armstrong on cornet (and then later on trumpet). The second line was no less distinguished with Lil Hardin Armstrong on piano and Johnny St. Cyr on banjo.

“Big Butter and Egg Man from the West”

“Big Butter and Egg Man from the West”

“Big Butter and Egg Man from the West” was recorded in 1926 in Chicago . The title was slang for a big spender that actually had little money.

The song in an A B C A form, with each section being eight bars long. Armstrong’s fourth chorus solo became very famous and was a model for many other Dixieland cornet players. Gunther Schuller claimed that “no composer, not even a Mozart or a Schubert, composed anything more natural and simply inspired” (Schuller 1968). We close with Dixieland again moving toward Swing.
