“Big Butter and Egg Man from the West”

“Big Butter and Egg Man from the West” was recorded in 1926 in Chicago . The title was slang for a big spender that actually had little money.

The song in an A B C A form, with each section being eight bars long. Armstrong’s fourth chorus solo became very famous and was a model for many other Dixieland cornet players. Gunther Schuller claimed that “no composer, not even a Mozart or a Schubert, composed anything more natural and simply inspired” (Schuller 1968). We close with Dixieland again moving toward Swing.

Follow the chart below as you listen to Louis Armstrong and His Hot Five’s “Big Butter and Egg Man from the West” (1926).

1st Chorus

Melody played by Armstrong with some improvisation. Both the clarinet and trombone improvise freely behind Armstrong throughout the 1 st chorus. The rhythm section plays a steady four-to-the-bar-pattern.

0:00-0:10 A 8mm

0:10-0:19 B 8mm

0:19-0:28 C 8mm

0:28-0: 37 A 8mm

2nd Chorus

Vocal (Alix) accompanied by rhythm section only. She sings lyrics to the same melody Armstrong played in the 1str Chorus. Piano and banjo get away from the strict four-to-the-bar pattern played in the 1st Chorus and start to play more freely.

0: 37-0: 47 A

Lyrics: “I want a Butter and Egg Man,

a way out on the West.”

 

0: 47-0:56 B

“‘Cause I’m getting tired of working all day,

I want somebody who wants me to play.”

 

0:56-1:06 C

“Pretty close has never been mine,

But if my dream comes true, the sun’s gonna shine.”

 

1:06-1:16 A

“‘Cause I want a Butter and Egg Man,

Want the great big Butter and Egg for me.”

 

3rd Chorus

Armstrong replies to Alix.

1:16-1:24 A and B Armstrong speaks the lyrics; he does not sing them.

“Here, here!

Now Mama I’m your Butter and Egg Man.

But I’m different Honey,

but I’m from way down in the South.

Now listen Baby,”

 

1:24-1:35 B

“I’ll buy you all the pretty things that you think you need,

A-a-as long I can keep this cornet up to my mouth.”

1:35-1:44 C Armstrong starts to mix speaking with singing.

(singing) “Oh-h-h, I’ll play you a little minor in G,

(speaking) And if you say it’s necessary Baby, I’ll even high C!” (laughs)

 

1:44-1:58 A

(singing) “‘Cause I’m your Big Butter and Egg Man,

(speaking) Come here Baby and kiss me!

Big Butter and Egg Man from way down South.”

 

4th Chorus

1:58-2:37 Trumpet solo. Armstrong solos with rhythm accompaniment only; no horns for an entire chorus. Piano and banjo keep strict four-to-the-bar pattern throughout.

5th Chorus

2:37-2:46 Starts with section C and Alix sings the lyrics to C:

“Pretty close has never been mine,

But if my dream comes true, the sun’s gonna shine.”

2:46 to end Horns play the last A section and Alix (vocals) drops out. Both the clarinet and trombone improvise freely behind Armstrong as in the 1st Chorus.
