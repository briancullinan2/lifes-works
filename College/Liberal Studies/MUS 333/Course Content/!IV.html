Introduction

Soprano saxophonist, composer, collaborator with Miles Davis, and influential jazz musician Wayne Shorter spoke recently, and ironically, of the musical experiments of the late 1960s and 1970s.  "Remember when the avant-garde came along, and all the guys were like (he grunts some notes).  The sound was like elephants.  They were calling on people to break the mold.  All to break free of the establishment!  Storm the walls!

"I don't want to fall into that trap of who's doing something new for newness sake.  For the sake of what?   I can say, 'What is the answer to life?' The question is.  I'm in a place now where I want to get all the stops out, but not run rampant with something like the avant-garde.  Neo this, neo that.  I want all the ingredients in there” (Koransky 2003, 61).

Things started to change radically in jazz at the end of the decade of the 1950s.  Miles, among others, began to pass up the rapidly changing chord changes of bop in order to slow things down and stretch it out.  Other experimenters, like Ornette Coleman, dropped the conceit of pre-planned tunes, chord changes, or even meter. 

The increasing turbulence of jazz in the late 1960s would give way to more radical fusion of jazz with funk and rock.  Performers were pulling at how jazz fundamentally was created and the changes brought forward in the late 1950s and early 1960s did overthrow the established norms that had been part of jazz conventions since the 1930s:  a swing feel, chord changes from songs and standards as the basis for improvisation, and virtuosity as one of primary aesthetic criterions for quality in improvisation. 

Expansion of the concept of the chord to encompass scale structures was developed most influentially by Miles and sidemen that were soon to become important solo artists in their own right, like saxophonist John Coltrane.  This new and expanded way of looking at the basic building blocks of jazz improvisation was eventually called modal jazz.

Freeing jazz from the tyranny of planned chord changes, standard tunes, and set meters developed from the work of Ornette Coleman and his groups.  This experimentation was named after a 1960 Coleman recording called free jazz. Modal and Free Jazz (late 1950s and beyond)

Miles Davis

In "So What," recorded by Miles' sextet on the album Kind of Blue (1959) and performed live by them at the time, Miles and Bill Evans developed a piece that is formally structured around a single shift from an opening mode to a second mode that is based on a pitch one half step away from the first mode with a later return to the original mode. Both modes in "So What" are built on the diatonic modal pattern known as Dorian (which hss an interval pattern of W H W W W H W). Dorian sounds a half step early on like the minor mode, characteristically sounds a half step between scale steps 6-7, and closes with a whole step for scale steps 7-8.

"So What" uses two Dorian modes built a half step from one another (on pitches D and Eb, respectively).

D Dorian

D     E     F   G    A    B    C    D

    W   H    W  W   W   H    W

Eb Dorian

Eb    F    Gb    Ab    Bb    C    Db    Eb

    W   H     W      W     W    H     W

"So What" opens with an Intro by solo bass. The form kicks in with eight measures in D Dorian, which is then repeated.  Eb Dorian is sounded for eight measures which gives way to a return to D Dorian for eight measures to close.  Basically the form is a 32 bar AABA or Song Form and is generated not by the tune or chord changes, but by modal changes which then spin out accompanimental harmonic and melodic figures or motives that are used throughout (dotted quarter-eight on beats three and four).

Form of "So What"

A      8mm         D Dorian

A      8mm         D Dorian

B      8mm         Eb Dorian

A      8mm         D Dorian

Again, what is important in modal jazz is that things are no longer based upon a series of chord changes. Rather, it is a modal scale form that defines a span of music.  And in the hands of Miles, things slowed and were built of repeating melodic and harmonic gestures.

"My Favorite Things" is in triple meter and was not normally the type of song with which jazz musicians would choose to work.  However, pianist McCoy Tyner said that "it was a song_plugger, I think, who brought us the sheet music [of "My Favorite Things"] at the Jazz Gallery. 

I didn't like the tune at first but John did, so we started playing it and it began to grow on me.  The audience really liked it, the way we played it in three-quarter time.  It was probably the only jazz waltz they'd ever heard since Sonny Rollins' 'Valse Hot.'  So John decided to record it on his next album" (Thomas [n.d.],133).

Coltrane and his quartet were soon in the studio in October of 1960 and recorded enough material for three albums: My Favorite Things, Coltrane Plays the Blues, and Coltrane's Sound.  Propelled by the title track, the album My Favorite Things sold more than 50,000 units during its first year, which was ten times that of a standard popular jazz album.  It was a jazz hit.

"My Favorite Things" was recorded with Coltrane on soprano sax, McCoy Tyner on piano, bassist Steve Davis, and Elvin Jones on drums.  The song is a waltz, or quicker-paced triple meter dance.  It features 16 measure sections of the theme or tune of "My Favorite Things" sounded between longer passages of improvisation.

The improvisational passages are based on two different modes:  E Major and E Dorian.  These, in turn, are spun out into a two chord modal accompanimental pattern (minor ninth chords on E and F#) by pianist McCoy Tyner.

E Major and E Dorian modes share common elements, yet distinct differences remain with Dorian having lowered 3rd, 4th, and 7th scale degrees compared to Major.

       W    W    H   W   W   W    H

E Major:   E   F#   G#   A   B   C#   D#   E

E Dorian:  E   F#   G     A   B   C#   D     E

       W     H    W   W   W   H   W

The alternation between the tune and modal section of improvisation was infinitely expandable.  Coltrane extended his three choruses into a near fourteen minute performance in My Favorite Things (1960).  On Afro Blue Impressions (1963) he recorded the song again in a performance of twenty-two minutes and there was even a three hour live performance of the song played without a break.

The tempo of "My Favorite Things" moves rapidly and it is easy to hear the triple meter as a single ONE consisting of three beats.  Listen closely for pianist McCoy Tyner’s alternation between two chords as he comps.  Note, too, the repetitive and drone-like bass line and in the Intro the ostinato that the rhythm section sets up and is used, with slight variations, throughout the piece.

In the second chorus, McCoy Tyner’s solo is played in a very pianistic and chordal style, rather than in a single note, horn-like line. His voicing of chords in fourths influenced many pianists that came after him.

In contrast to Tyner’s approach, Coltrane’s solo in the third chorus is filled with rapid, scalar passages that demonstrate not only incredible virtuosity, but also a knowledge and command of harmony that no jazz player at the time had even begun to approach.  Coltrane’s influence is still being felt today over thirty-five years after his death.

"Alabama" (1963)

On a Sunday morning in September of 1963, the Ku Klux Klan bombed the 16th Street Baptist Church in Birmingham, Alabama killing four young girls ages eleven to fourteen.  At the funeral, the Reverend Martin Luther King, Jr. delivered a eulogy to over 8,000 mourners and to a national television audience that included John Coltrane. Deeply affected by the girls’ murder and moved by Dr. King’s speech, Coltrane composed "Alabama."

"Alabama" is based on a single mode, Aeolian, which has the same interval structure as the natural minor scale.

Modal Jazz

The formation of modal jazz was worked out by Miles and pianist Bill Evans in a series of late 1950s records, Milestones (1958), Sketches of Spain (1959-60), and most famously in Kind of Blue (1959).

In Kind of Blue, Miles recorded with an all-star sextet that featured Julian 'Cannonball' Adderly on alto sax, the great John Coltrane on tenor sax, the influential composer and pianist Bill Evans, Paul Chambers on bass, and James Cobb on drums.  We will discuss Kind of Blue in much more detail later in the course in Topic 4, but for now let's talk about some of the basic things that made modal jazz new and distinctive.

Introduction

Miles and pianist Bill Evans began to experiment with alternating whole scales rather than a series of chords as a basis for improvisation and, further, to generate form in jazz.  The rapid chord changes in bop, where a rapidly changing series of chords was used as the basis for improvisation, had increasingly become less attractive to many jazz musicians as the emphasis became increasingly focused on technical and virtuosic display.

With modal jazz, more so than the actual pitch content of the scales used, the fact that rapid harmonic changes were radically slowed became striking and influential.  Musicians call the rate at which chords or the harmony changes the 'harmonic rhythm' of a piece.  Harmonic rhythm slowed from the nearly one chord change to the beat (or in the case of the virtuosic late bop improvisation of Coltrane where he substituted three chords in the space of every single chord in the standard progression) to where performers could settle into a mode for a whole section of a piece.  Coltrane, performing modal works in contrast to his late bop performances, would even improvise in a single mode for three hours without a break in one famous live performance.  Further, changes in mode could also dictate structural change in a work, or signal that the group was moving from one part of the form to the next.

The word mode goes back quite far in Western music.  At root the term encompasses many of the other scale patterns of whole and half steps—of diatonic scales, for example—that go beyond major and minor scales.  Miles and his collaborators expanded the concept of the chord (three or four pitch aggregates drawn from a scale) to include all seven pitches of a diatonic scale.  Some of the new modal jazz scales explored scale constructions other cultures such as India and Spain, most prominently.

As a practical matter when accompanying a soloist, rhythm section musicians (like the pianist and bass player) would often localize and emphasize parts of the scale.  There are famous recorded examples of modal works from the late 1950s through the mid-1960s by pianists Bill Evans (with Miles) and McCoy Tyner (with John Coltrane) where they alternate between two chordal areas culled from the full mode.  Bass players often focused on repeating patterns (a process which will be radicalized further in later fusion styles with the adoption of explicitly repetitive funk and rock bass lines), or the sustained emphasis on a single pitch called a drone. Free Jazz (1960)

Free Jazz (1960)

John Litweiler wrote that Free Jazz is "a series of collective improvisations separated by composed themes, at first fanfares, with each horn in turn taking the lead and the others improvising ongoing responses at will, ending in bass, then drums, duets.  For most of the work’s forty minutes the basses play an up-down, hugga-bugga shuffle rhythm" (Litweiler 1994, 96). 

Unlike  Miles’s 1960 modal album, Kind of Blue, Free Jazz does not use modes or a primary pitch center (although thought the music does gravitate to certain pitch centers for fixed periods of time).

The album features two piano-less quartets (eight musicians in total), yet we rarely hear all eight players performing together.

"Lonely Woman" (1959)

In 1959, the same year as the Miles Davis album Kind of Blue, Ornette Coleman turned the jazz world on its ear with his Atlantic recording The Shape of Jazz to Come (Atlantic CD 1317).  The songs abandoned both chords and standard form entirely!  

"Lonely Woman" from The Shape of Jazz remains conventional in that it has a melody that is played both at the beginning end of the piece and the performance and the solos are accompanied by a traditional jazz four-to-the-bar rhythmic pulse.  However, there is no preset harmonic, or little or no measure structure.  The quartet’s 1959 appearance in New York City at the famous Blue Note club created a love-hate reaction to his music.  Traditional jazz musicians thought the music was junk, while forward looking players thought that it was truly the shape of jazz to come.  The fact that Ornette was playing a yellow plastic alto sax at the time only added to the controversy.

"Lonely Woman" features Ornette on alto sax, Donald Cherry on trumpet, bassist Charlie Haden, and Billy Higgins on drums.  The song is composed and performed in a rather loose, AABA form.  Its dirge-like melody coupled with the frantic rhythm section accompaniment evokes the feelings and frustration that the title implies.  In fact, the rhythm section is playing double the time of the much slower-moving melody and we hear Ornette playing sounding nearly like a person crying out in pain.

While listening to this piece and the others that follow in this section, try to set aside any preconceived notions of how you think that jazz should sound.  Each piece, from "Lonely Woman" through the Cecil Taylor video and Cotrane’s late free jazz recording becomes increasingly freer, abandoning all of the rules.

Cecil Taylor

Cecil Taylor

Cecil Taylor remains one of the most innovative of the 1960s free jazz generation of musicians.  Taylor’s music often moves beyond the narrow limits of what some believe is and is not jazz.

Read the article on Cecil Taylor.

Taylor’s music, starting in the late 1960s, put great demands on many of its listeners as it moved into the realm of avant-garde classical art music.  His prodigious piano technique sounded complex clusters of pitches, dense and irregular phrasing, and rhythms not common found in jazz.  In his emphasis on musical textures, rather than musical lines, generating layers or groups of tightly packed staccato (short and dry) figures played in a percussive manner, and with some sections of his improvisations that no longer function in a key or mode (atonal), his improvisations move very close to the improvisations found in art music at the time by the likes of Karlheinz Stockhausen, Luciano Berio, and Lucas Foss.

Late Coltrane

Late Coltrane

As we move into the mid-1960s, John Coltrane began to explore increasingly radical and emotional means of musical expression.  He was working on the edge of the jazz avant-garde.  However, unlike many other 1960s experimenters, he continued to attract a large following.

Pianist McCoy Tyner left Coltrane and his quartet in 1966 and was replaced by Alice McLeod (Coltrane), his second wife.  He also added a second tenor sax player to the group, the innovative Pharaoh Sanders, as well as a second drummer, Rashied Ali, and several African-influenced percussionists.  He would, at times, feature up to three tenor saxes, two alto saxes, and two basses in his group, in addition to those mentioned above.

In Coltrane’s late work (1965-67), his interest in tonal organization and even playing specific pitches waned.  He and Sanders began to exploit and use radical timbres that more resembled human cries.  Textures became increasingly dense and full of sustained dissonances all moving within a loose structure.  He and his group pursued collective improvisation that emphasized texture and register (highest range of the sax).

At the same time, his later work also became much more overtly spiritual and meditative in albums such as Om (1965), Mediaitions (1965), and Intersellar Space (1967).

"Mars" from Interstellar Space was recorded shortly before his death in 1967.  This is a duet album, with only Coltrane on sax (and bells) accompanied by Rashied Ali on drums.  Coltrane "always played off the drums," said Rashied Ali.  "He told me the drums freed him, he didn’t have to worry about chord changes.  He got the freedom to play what he wanted from working directly off the drums" (Thomas [n.d.],202).

Influence of Free Jazz

John Coltrane's death "created chaos in the 'free thing' because he was its leader," wrote Miles Davis in his autobiography.  "He was like Bird [Charlie Parker] to all those musicians who considered themselves 'out'—you know, 'free,' out in space: he was like a god to them.  When he died it was much like when Bird died for a lot of bebop musicians who looked to him for directions, even though he himself had been directionless for a lot of years.  Ornette Coleman was still around, and some turned to him.  But for most Trane [Coltrane] had been their guiding light, and after he was gone they seemed to me like people in a boat in the ocean without compass or paddles.  It seemed a lot of what he stood for musically died with him.  Even though some of his disciples carried on his message, they carried it to smaller and smaller audiences" (Davis 1989,286). 

By the early 1970s, free jazz indeed seemed to be disappearing amid the rush to embrace fusion.  Yet, it revived late in that decade as Ornette Coleman put a new free jazz group together, Old and New Dreams, and other long-standing free jazz groups, such as Sun Ra's Arkestra and the Art Ensemble of Chicago, found new listeners.

New, gifted, and innovative musicians, like Anthony Braxton and Anthony Davis, came into the style and continued to erode the boundaries between free jazz and the contemporary European art music of Karlheinz Stockhausen, Bern Alois Zimmermann, and others.   Electric guitarist Pat Metheny recorded with Ornette Coleman in a free style (1985-86).  There even developed a free funk hybrid between free jazz, hard rock, and funk fusion in the late 1970s through 1980s at the hands of electric guitarist, singer, and composer James 'Blood' Ulmer. Free jazz

Free jazz

Free jazz is a label attached to a particular style of avant-garde jazz in the 1960s.  The name was derived from the title of an influential 1960 album, Free Jazz, by saxophonist Ornette Coleman and his group.  In addition to Coleman, free jazz became associated with a wide range of work from diverse artists such as pianist Cecil Taylor, tenor saxophonist Albert Ayler, and later John Coltrane.

On the face of things, free jazz performances rejected the conventions of bop and cool jazz from the 1950s: fast, complex, and busy improvisational lines, rapid chord changes, or the return to arrangements in cool jazz.  Free jazz rejected predetermined chord progressions and the chorus structure of jazz form.  Group improvisation was cultivated within shifting and looser forms.  Shifts in the performance were built around predetermined signals—melodic gestures, even nods of the head.   Standard meters, such as duple meter in four beats to the bar, could be shunned or several meters could be implied in the mix that was created.

Many performers began to push the envelope in terms of what was acceptable as musical sound.  Many pushed for more overt emotional expression in imitation of the human voice (shrieks, moans, yells). 

Free jazz ensembles generally did not use a piano, since the instrument was the main chording or harmony instrument in jazz at the time.  Electronic instruments and electronic manipulation was usually avoided in free jazz, since many were interested in working with textures, rather than melodies.  Often free jazz pieces feature a great deal of turbulence and the style opened jazz up to the influences of  a variety of world music, including African and Indian music.

For all of this, many free jazz performances did devolve back upon convention.  Many returned to four beats to the bar over a steady tempo.  On often hears distinct solo or melody sections backed by accompaniment.  There were even prearranged melodies that were used.

While Ornette Coleman is widely credited for showcasing free jazz ideas through his landmark recordings and performances, there were earlier free jazz efforts by pianist Lennie Tristano on recordings such as “Intuition” and “Digression” that were made nearly a decade earlier in 1949.  While Tristano’s efforts with saxophonist Lee Konitz in playing without a predetermined theme or harmonic pattern were conceptually revolutionary and in line with what would later be considered free jazz, the results sounded much closer to contemporary bebop than the boiling, over-the-top performances by Ornette and others.
