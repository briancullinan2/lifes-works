Kansas City Swing

Introduction

At the same time Lunceford, Redman, Ellington and the society bandleaders were crafting arrangements for their bands and featured soloists, something different was going on in Kansas City, Missouri. In contrast to the complex arrangements of the eastern bands, the Kansas City brand of swing music evolved around what became known as ‘head’ arrangements. A head arrangement is a type of arrangement developed and fine-tuned by the players in the band during a performance and one that is not written down by one person - the arranger. As a result, the players (aka sideman) in a Kansas City band had a much greater impact on the sound of the band than their eastern counterparts. Because of the looser compositional format, the focal point of the music turned to the soloist and away from the arranger. The success of a Kansas City band was dependent upon the strength of its players.

The twelve-bar blues form and the A A B A song form with blues-tinged chord progressions were the most popular vehicles for improvisation. Kansas City jazz musicians developed riff-based arrangements based on simple, short syncopated ideas that were played in unison by the section voicing the riff. These riffs were played behind both the melody and the solos. New riffs were composed spontaneously as the inspiration struck the players. Many of the riffs were so popular that they became songs themselves! Count Basie’s theme song, “One O’clock Jump,” started out as a riff.

From the late 1920s to the late 1930s Kansas City was run by Tom Pendergast, an alderman, who opened the city to liquor, gambling, and prostitution. This, in combination with the end of Prohibition and the Depression, turned Kansas City into a freewheeling, no-holds-barred town that supported over 300 nightclubs.
