Virtuosity

Read the article on Art Tatum.

Art Tatum possessed one of the most remarkable virtuosic talents in jazz. Classical (art music) pianists regularly attended his concerts. Watch a performance of Art Tatum.

Tatum’s 1949 recorded version of Ann Ronnell’s popular song, “Willow Weep for Me,” is a breathtaking show piece. He takes repetitive sections of the song as a license for further reworking, yet helping create an almost hypnotic effect. The piece opens with a 4 bar vamp, whose time is pushed and pulled (an expressive technique called ‘rubato’ by musicians). Tatum also alters the character of each chorus which allows him to continue to vary the piece, yet still retain a larger sense of formal coherence. In “ Willow, Weep for Me” the first chorus has a very George Gershwin sound, composer of many poplar songs, American in Paris, and Rhapsody in Blue. The second chorus, in contrast, emphasizes the blues underpinning of the original song. Tatum’s performance follows the 32 bar song form of the original, with the final A section of the second chorus also serving as a double-time coda.

Follow the chart below as you listen to Art Tatum’s “ Willow Weep for Me” (1949).

0:00-0:10 4 Intro vamp

1st chorus

0:10-0:28 6+2 A original melody is paraphrased by Tatum, all played over the Intro’s vamp

0:28-0:46 6+2 A modulation at end of section leading to the bridge

0:46-1:07 2+2+2+2 B virtuosic runs

1:07-1:27 6+2 A return of paraphrased them over vamp

2nd chorus

1:27-1:49 2+2+2+2 A blues harmonies emphasized; alternates between single and double time

1:49-2:11 2+2+2+2 A single-double time alternation continues

2:11-2:32 2+2+2+2 B complex rhythms and virtuosic passagework

2:32 to end 4+2+2+2 A coda: tempo quickens, adds 2 bars; this almost becomes a separate piece
