“West End Blues”

Click for a transcription of Louise Armstrong's solos in “ West End Blues.”

Follow the chart below and listen to Louis Armstrong and His Hot Five’s “West End Blues” (1928).

Introduction

0:00-0:13 Armstrong plays an unaccompanied solo filled with rhythmic twists and turns. Armstrong explores the entire range of the instrument.

0:13-0:16 A full band chord sets up the melody

1st Chorus

0:16-0:50 Armstrong states the melody in a dominant manner accompanied by (1) the clarinet playing a harmony part a third below the melody for most of the chorus with a fast vibrato, (2) the trombone playing sustained notes with a slide up from one note to the next, and (3) the piano and banjo playing heavy four-to-the bar chords. No percussion yet.

2nd Chorus

0:50-1:24 Trombone solo (Robinson). The solo characterized by the many slides (aka glissandi). The piano and banjo continue playing heavy, four-to-the bar chords. The drummer (Singleton) joins in playing rhythmic patterns on what sounds like milk bottles! Jazz historian Mark C. Gridley identifies this unique-sounding instrument as a hand-held version of the Bock-A-Da-Bock, which consists of two, three inch cymbals that are fixed close to one another and are played like the Spoons (Gridley,2000:70). 

3rd Chorus

1:24-1:59 Clarinetist (Strong) and now vocalist Armstrong engage in a call and response chorus. The clarinet starts out playing a one bar melody in the low register that is repeated by Armstrong. As the call and response pattern continues, Armstrong’s responses become more and more rhythmically and melodically complex while the clarinet calls remain rhythmically and melodically simple. 

4th Chorus

1:59-2:10 Unaccompanied piano solo (Hines). For the first four bars, Hines plays florid, right hand lines while keeping the four-to-the bar chords going in his left hand.

2:10-2:21 In the second four bars the character of the piano solo changes dramatically as Hines begins plying a more pronounced stride style with his left hand and a series of hard-edged octave runs with his right.

2:21-2:32 For the last four bars of the chorus Hines returns to the florid style heard in the first four bars.

5th Chorus

2:32-2:44 Trumpet solo (Armstrong), along with Strong and Robinson hold notes for the first four bars. Piano and banjo enter with the four-to-the bar chords.

2:44-2:56 In the second four bars Armstrong plays a double-time solo line while the clarinet and trombone continue with their sustained slightly more syncopated lines.

Ending

2:56-3:04 Unaccompanied piano plays a series of descending arpeggios.

3:04 to end Armstrong plays a short melodic phrase at a slower tempo that leads into the three chords the end the piece. The drummer has his final say with two short notes at the very end.
