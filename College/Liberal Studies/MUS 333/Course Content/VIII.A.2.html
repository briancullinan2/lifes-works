The ‘swing’ in Swing jazz

Swing jazz has a distinct feel that carried through into Bebop and still is cultivated by most contemporary performers. Some modern jazz performs, like Cecil Taylor, dropped the swing feel developed during the 1930s, but most jazz performers still maintain a vestige of swing feeling in their music.

Swing makes the music move, jump, and pop. It was a great style to use to accompany dancing and one to sell to audiences and listeners either in clubs, through radio broadcasts, or on recordings. Swing makes the music active and very kinetic.

If we listen again to Scott Joplin’s performance of “Mapleleaf Rag,” notice how often Joplin divides the beat into four subdivisions. You can see this in the Joplin ’s published sheet music of “Mapleleaf Rag,” as well.

Listen to Scott Joplin’s performance of “Mapleleaf Rag” (1898) for the duple (4 beat subdivision) on each beat.

Swing, though, cultivated a different feel that goes down into the level of the subdivision of a beat. In Swing, the beat is roughly subdivided into groups of threes (triple subdivision), with the first two subdivisions bound together so that a long-short pattern is sounded.
