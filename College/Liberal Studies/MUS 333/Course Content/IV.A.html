Introduction

Soprano saxophonist, composer, collaborator with Miles Davis, and influential jazz musician Wayne Shorter spoke recently, and ironically, of the musical experiments of the late 1960s and 1970s.  "Remember when the avant-garde came along, and all the guys were like (he grunts some notes).  The sound was like elephants.  They were calling on people to break the mold.  All to break free of the establishment!  Storm the walls!

"I don't want to fall into that trap of who's doing something new for newness sake.  For the sake of what?   I can say, 'What is the answer to life?' The question is.  I'm in a place now where I want to get all the stops out, but not run rampant with something like the avant-garde.  Neo this, neo that.  I want all the ingredients in there‚Äù (Koransky 2003, 61).

Things started to change radically in jazz at the end of the decade of the 1950s.  Miles, among others, began to pass up the rapidly changing chord changes of bop in order to slow things down and stretch it out.  Other experimenters, like Ornette Coleman, dropped the conceit of pre-planned tunes, chord changes, or even meter. 

The increasing turbulence of jazz in the late 1960s would give way to more radical fusion of jazz with funk and rock.  Performers were pulling at how jazz fundamentally was created and the changes brought forward in the late 1950s and early 1960s did overthrow the established norms that had been part of jazz conventions since the 1930s:  a swing feel, chord changes from songs and standards as the basis for improvisation, and virtuosity as one of primary aesthetic criterions for quality in improvisation. 

Expansion of the concept of the chord to encompass scale structures was developed most influentially by Miles and sidemen that were soon to become important solo artists in their own right, like saxophonist John Coltrane.  This new and expanded way of looking at the basic building blocks of jazz improvisation was eventually called modal jazz.

Freeing jazz from the tyranny of planned chord changes, standard tunes, and set meters developed from the work of Ornette Coleman and his groups.  This experimentation was named after a 1960 Coleman recording called free jazz.
