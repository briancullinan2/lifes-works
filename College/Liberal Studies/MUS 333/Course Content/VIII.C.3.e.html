New York Swing

Alvino Rey and Larry Clinton

While Miller, the Dorsey brothers, and Whitman included some good improvised, ‘hot’ jazz in their performances, bandleaders Alvino Rey and Larry Clinton catered to the dancers. Along with bandleaders Vincent Lopez, Hal Kemp and Kay Kyser, the music of Rey and Clinton fit firmly into the sweet or society band mold. This music is anything but ‘hot!’

Alvino Rey was one of the first musicians to play the steel guitar in jazz and you will see him playing one in the next video. Rey hired some of the finest arrangers around, Neal Hefti, Ray Conniff, Johnny Mandel, and Billy May, all of whom went on to have big careers in the music business. His version of the pop song, “The Whistler and His Dog,” is a novelty song complete with sound effects, funny facial gestures, and a calm, easy steel guitar solo by Rey himself. The King Sisters were his vocal group.

Watch and listen to Alvino Rey, “The Whistler and His Dog” (1940)

Larry Clinton first made it in the music business as an arranger for many of the top bands in the 1930s. He worked at various times for Tommy and Jimmy Dorsey and the Casa Loma Orchestra (a band that was considered a cooperative band, as there has no leader).

Clinton composed and arranged “The Dipsy Doodle” for Tommy Dorsey, which became a huge hit in 1936. Dorsey gave Clinton credit for the song every time it was played and, as a result, Clinton became so well known that he started his own band in 1937.

Clinton loved to adapt classical melodies for his band. His hit, “My Reverie,” is taken from a melody written by the Impressionist art music composer Claude Debussy. In the video you will see and hear restrained playing and a straightforward vocal by Bea Wain – pure, unadulterated sweet music.
