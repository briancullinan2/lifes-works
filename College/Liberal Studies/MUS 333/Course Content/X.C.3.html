“Carolina Shout” (2)

“Carolina Shout” goes way beyond Ragtime as Johnson uses what were called ‘crushed’ thirds (playing the third scale degree from both the major minor scale at the same time), mixing in triple subdivisions (a swing feel), and an intense syncopation between bass line and treble all coupled with an incredible rhythmic drive.

“Carolina Shout” four beats to the bar. The song is divided up more by harmonic (chords) than by melodic (melody) phrases. The form is A A B C C D B D.

Follow the chart below as you listen to James P. Johnson’s “ Carolina Shout” (1921).

0:00-0:05 Introduction

0:05-0:25 A 16mm Typical stride piano left hand accompaniment. Bass notes are played on beats one and three while chords are played on beats two and four with only occasional variation.

0:25-0:45 A repeat 16mm Melody is played up a third with embellishments. Listen to how Johnson changes the left hand bass note and chord pattern. By diverging from the standard bass note and chord pattern, Johnson creates a lop-sided left hand pattern that at first gives the impression that the CD is skipping! You have to listen hard to the melody to know where you are in the song.

0:45-1:05 B 16mm New harmonic and melodic material.

1:05-1:24 C 16mm The same harmonies as B, however, with a more aggressive bass pattern and melody.

1:24-1:43 C repeat 16mm Same as above with a few embellishments.

1:43-2:01 D 16mm New harmonic and melodic material. Johnson again improvises on the typical stride left hand pattern.

2:01-2:20 B 16mm Same harmonies in left hand with improvisation in right hand.

2:20-2:39 D Same harmonies with similar melodic material.

2:39 to end Tag 4mm to end.
