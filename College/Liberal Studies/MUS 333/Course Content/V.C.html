Lennie Tristano

Lennie Tristano (piano), Lee Konitz (alto sax)' Billy Bauer (guitar); Arnold Fishkin (bass); Shelley Manne (drums)

Pianist Lennie Tristano and alto sax player Lee Konitz (of The Birth of the Cool recording fame) were two players who, unfortunately, received little or no recognition from the general public. They were considered "musician's musicians" and plied their craft in and around New York City.

Tristano's music featured unusual, often complex melodies and harmonies played with a rhythm section (bass & drums) than was relegated to a subdued, strict time keeping role, quite different from the often flamboyant bop rhythm sections of the day. To quote Martin Schowten in his notes to the Capitol LP "Crosscurrents", "Lennie's [Tristano] select coterie of musicians [Konitz (alto sax), Warne Marsh (tenor sax), and Billy Bauer (guitar)] developed a low-temperature [read "cool"] approach to composition and improvisation… In terms of the dynamic level, nothing much was allowed to happen …"

Tristano, blind from infancy, was also a prominent jazz educator whose teachings influenced many, many players in the early 50's, among them, Lee Konitz. Konitz came to prominence playing with Claude Thornhill's band in 1947 the same year he joined Miles Davis, Gil Evans Gerry Mulligan in the project that created The Birth of the Cool recordings. All of this while he was just 20 years old! He became a teacher to students all over the world and has won many jazz polls as best alto player.

"Subconscious Lee" is a Lee Konitz composition based on the chord progression of Cole Porter's "What Is This Thing Called Love," and has an AABA form, each section 8 measures long. (It was and is common practice for jazz composers to borrow a chord progression that he or she likes and to write a new melody over it.) Take a look at the melody and notice that in every A section the melody is different but the chords remain the same. While the solos in this particular recording aren't quite as "low-temperature" as Mr. Schowten suggests, they do, however, possess less drive, rhythmic variety and emotional content then their Bop counterparts.
