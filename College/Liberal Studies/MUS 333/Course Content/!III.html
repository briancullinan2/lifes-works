Introduction

In the late 1960s, experiments by Miles Davis and others in fusing jazz—or mixing it together with African-American and pop traditions like funk and rock—became increasingly popular with audiences. Part of a larger trend in popular music which fused funk and roots reggae, rock and art music, and folk with everything, jazz fusion began to again attract larger listening audiences to jazz.

The last time that jazz had a mass consumer audience was during the 1940s, when swing was the popular music of the day. While fusion never reached swing's height of popularity, it still was the best thing to come along for jazz musicians' bank accounts in a long time.

Fusion was not embraced by everyone and there was open hostility in the jazz press, in particular, towards innovators like Miles Davis. In some instances, it has taken nearly thirty years for some of the early fusion work to gain influence in jazz.

As is always the case, too, that while we may be focusing on the latest innovations and innovators in jazz, others jazz styles carried on doing interesting things. While fusion became popular, other jazz styles like bebop, modal, and even dixieland catered to their own audiences who patronized jazz artists that continued to perform and record in that style. Fusion (late 1960s to the 1990s)

Fusion (late 1960s to the 1990s)

From the late 1960s through the 1990s, jazz has been fused with a variety of musical styles. In this section of the course, we will focus on those fusion styles& mdash;funk, rock, and club sound—that were both dominant and most influential at the time.

Jazz-funk fusion is a mixing of jazz with funk to create a more electric and popularly-infused style of jazz. Jazz funk fusion is generally in duple meter and is characterized by highly syncopated and complex instrumental rhythm section work that frequently creates polyrhythms.

Read the article on jazz-rock fusion. We will discuss club sounds in more detail later in this section of the course.

Most instruments used in jazz funk and rock fusion are electric (electric guitar, electric bass, electric piano, and synthesizer). The soprano sax—which can cut through the dense electronics more easily—is used much more than alto or tenor.Both styles frequently use vamps, like in funk and rock, while jazz standards and standard jazz musical forms are not used. The emphasis is no longer on improvised solos and the texture in both styles can be quite dense at times. Drummers frequently accent every beat, rather than the beats two and four as in other styles of jazz. Neither jazz funk or rock swings and both styles are played in a straight-ahead manner. While funk, itself, is frequently vocal music backed by a hot instrumental rhythm section, jazz funk (and rock) is almost exclusively instrumental. Listen for these style characteristics when you hear different pieces in this section of the course.

Miles Davis, so characteristically, was not the first to fuse jazz with rock or funk. Experiments by Gary Burton, Soft Machine, and Miles' own late 1960s drummer Tony Williams' group Lifetime were recording jazz fusion before Miles, but Miles certainly took the new trend and did remarkable things with it. Bitches Brew (1969-70)

Bitches Brew (1969-70)

Miles' landmark double-LP recording, Bitches Brew, really brought jazz fusion experiments to the forefront. The name of the album was said to be from a desert on a menu at Serendipity, a then-famous New York retaurant.

Teo Macero, Miles' producer and head recording engineer in the late 1960s, helped Miles mix down albums from long studio jams and live concert tapes. Bitches Brew, alone, was edited down from nine hours of studio tapes. Read about Teo Macero.

Even though two three-minute 45s were issued from the ten to twenty-minute pieces on Bitches Brew, this album—and In a Silent Way (1969) that preceded—it were much different than jazz recordings that came before them.

In Bitches Brew, listeners had to adjust to the fact that solo improvisation was not at the heart of these fusion works and could no longer be considered the measure of quality for a jazz performance. The album featured a great deal of repetition, like that found in funk grooves and in pop music, and the standard musical forms used in jazz up to this time were discarded and the influence of art or classical music can be heard on the many through composed forms on the album.

While Bitches Brew was created as a collaborative effort by Miles' musicians performing together in the studio, the mix created by Teo Macero created a remarkably new soundscape. "Sometimes you subtract, take away the rhythm and leave just the high sound," said Miles, "or take what you know belongs to somebody else and keep the feeling" (Szwed 2002, 289-90). Electronica pioneer Brian Eno said that Macero in Bitches Brew "did something that was extremely modern, something that you can only do on records, which is, he took the performance to pieces, spatially. Now, those things were done by a group of musicians in a room, all sitting close to one another . . . but they were all close-miked, which meant that their sounds were quite separate from one another. And when Teo Macero mixed the record, he put them miles apart. So this is very interesting to listen to a music, where you have the conga player three streets down the road here, you have the trumpet player on a mountain over there, the guitar player—you have to look through binoculars to see him, you know! Everybody is far away, and so the impression that you have immediately is not that you are in a little place with a group of people playing, but that you're on a huge plateau and all of these things are going on sort of almost on the horizon, I think. And there's no attempt made by Teo Macero to make them connect with one another. In fact, he deliberately disconnects them from one another" (Szwed 2002, 298).

In particular, listen to the opening of the title track of Bitches Brew. It starts with bass vamps that lead to electronic piano 'explosions.' Overtop of this Miles enters and plays with extended and heavy use of an Echoplex that creates reverberation to the point that he virtually plays duets with his own echoed sound. Miles even works in an allusion to the Columbia Record's hit "Spinning Wheel" by Blood, Sweat, and Tears (Columbia was his long-time record label and the one that released Bitches Brew). At 2:51 in "Bitches Brew," another vamp kicks in and so on throughout the piece.

Listen to Miles Davis' "Bitches Brew" (1969).

The album Bitches Brew was constructed with the help of Miles' regular Quintet that he performed with live and with whom he worked things out for the recording. However, for the album's sessions he doubled things up so that he had basically had a double Quintet (and sometimes more):

Miles Davis, trumpet

Wayne Shorter, soprano sax and Benny Maupin, bass clarinet

Chick Corea and Joe Zawinul, electric pianos; with Larry Young, electric piano

John McLaughlin, electric guitar

Dave Holland and Harvey Brooks, electric and acoustic bass

Jack DeJohnette and Lenny White, drums; with Jumma Santos (Jim Riley) and Airto Moreira, percussion; and Charlie Alias, drums

This is a remarkably talented group of younger jazz musicians. Miles was always able to spot and attract the best of quickly-rising talent. Chick Corea, who played keyboards with Miles from 1968-70, was using an electronic piano on the Bitches Brew sessions and live with Miles' Quintet, rather than an acoustic piano. Corea further modified the sound with an Echoplex (echo generator) and a ring modulator (an early form of analog synthesizing). Chick, incidentally, thought that Bitchs Brew was merely a slower, more conservative version of the material that they performed regularly live with Miles' Quintet (Szwed 2002, 296). We will discuss Chick Corea, as well as guitarist John McLaughlin, in more detail later in this section of the course.

Austrian jazz composer and keyboardist Joe Zawinul, who played with Miles from the late 1960s into the early 1970s, provided several compositions for the Bitches Brew sessions including "Pharaoh's Dance."

Zawinul's "Pharaoh's Dance" is a programmatic work, in that it was written to tell a story in music. "Pharaoh's Dance" refers to the building of the pyramids. "It begins in a busy, animated spirit, with separate instrumental lines staggering in one after another, overlapping, piling waves of sound on top of quietly bustling percussion," writes John Szwed, "while Benny Maupin's bass clarinet bubbles up from below" (Szwed 2002, 298). You can also clearly hear the influence of rock on this track.

Listen to Miles Davis' "Pharaoh's Dance" (1969).

Bassist Dave Holland played with Miles from 1968-70 and, on Bitches Brew, plays vamps relentlessly. "A lot of people went into shock when they heard the group,& quot; said Dave Holland, "because what we were doing with electronics just wasn't a familiar sound. It was like having someone talk to you in a foreign language" (Szwed 2002, 289). Read about Dave Holland.

Miles' Quintet drummer, Jack DeJohnette who was with Miles from 1969-72, anchored the various drummers and percussionists on the Bitches Brew session. Miles said that he was in charge of the rhythm section because he was wearing sunglasses. Along with hard rock drummer Lenny White, DeJohnette was striking on every beat, rather than a more standard swinging style of drumming which even more evoked pop music in the sessions. Read about Jack DeJohnette.

Not only were the Bitches Brew sessions remarkable in their results, but the instruments that Miles' band members used was also setting a trend. Gone was the acoustic piano and it was replaced by two electronic pianos with early analog synthesizer modifications. Dave Holland's acoustic bass was supplemented by Harvey Brooks' Fender electric bass. (Brooks was a CBS record producer who also played on sessions for Bob Dylan and recorded with Electric Flag.) Wayne Shorter played soprano sax which could cut through the dense fabric of the ensemble or the electronics better than the lower-pitched alto or tenor sax. The use of two or more drummers, plus assorted percussionists, was also ahead of the curve.

Most of the pieces on Bitches Brew (1969-70) and its artistic predecessor, In a Silent Way (1969), were very focused on texture, or the 'weave' of sound. While Miles clearly directed and led things by his trumpet playing, the texture was generated more by the ensemble as a whole. So, the focus was on the ebb and flow of texture rather than the more standard focus of a soloist improvising with subsidiary accompaniment.

Listen to Miles' wonderful track "Shhh/Peaceful" from In a Silent Way (1969) that paved the way for Bitches Brew.

As discussed earlier, many of the pieces on Bitches Brew were generated over vamps. Some were also no more than sketches by Miles of a few chords that he wanted to hear voiced by the various members of his band. While there is the flavor of funk or rock fused into In a Silent Way or Bitches Brew, they are really much more complex than any contemporary funk or rock recording. Also, Miles disregarded the funk and rock radio friendly three minute format by recording pieces that lasted from ten to twenty some minutes each.

In the Bitches Brew sessions, usually one of the pair (or more) of the drummers, pianists, or bassists would lay down a groove and the other(s) would add to it or play in and around it. In "Bitches Brew," which you listened to earlier, there are also some fifteen edits in the final mix. Some are tape loops created by Miles and Teo Macero that were used to extend and create vamps from small gestures that were recorded in the session, but then were extended in the mixdown.

Miles performed "Miles Runs the Voodoo Down" often live with his Quintet, so it was fairly well formed when they took it into the studio. The title was added to the song by Columbia (and it is a title that Miles always hated).

Listen to Miles Davis, "Miles Runs the Voodoo Down" (1969).

 

On the Corner (1972)

On the Corner (1972)

In his 1972 album On the Corner, named after the corner that a young Miles could see from his home behind his father's dentist office in East St. Louis, Miles said that "I was trying to play the music that I grew up on now, that roadhouse, honky-tonk, funky thing that people used to dance to on Friday and Saturday nights" (Szwed 2002, 325). John Szwed comments that Miles wanted to "thicken the groove by filling in the space where harmonic variation would have been in conventional jazz" (Szwed 2002, 326).

The album was panned by critics and even fellow musicians. Stan Getz ripped the album by saying that the "music is worthless. It means nothing. There is no form, no content, and it barely swings. The soloists are playing a half tone above and a half tone below so it'll sound modern, but there's nothing to build on or anything logical—nothing" (Milkowski 1993, 6). Getz and other critics were behind the curve and it took almost a generation for jazz musicians to catch up with what Miles was doing on this album.

In On the Corner, Miles was pushing things even further than on Bitches Brew. The funk grooves are more pronounced and Miles is starting to use a wah-wah pedal (usually found in a guitarist's set up) on his amplified trumpet. The mix can be quite dense and he used a large number of musicians on the two days of recording sessions. At this time, Miles was obsessively listening to Sly and the Family Stone's "In Time" and the album was heavily influenced by it and the music of James Brown (funk)—of whom Miles was a great fan—rock guitarist Jimi Hendrix, and avant-garde art music composer Karlheinz Stockhausen.

We are going to listen to two cuts from On the Corner: "Black Satin" and the title track "On the Corner." In "Black Satin," after an Intro by tabla, sitar, and synthesizer (they also close the piece with an Outro), the groove kicks in and you can hear both a bass vamp and a strong James Brown influence on the recording since Miles' drummer lifted his lick from James Brown's recording of "Cold Sweat" (Milkowski 1993, 7).Miles' trumpet—which plays the melody once the groove kicks in—is both amplified and run through a wah-wah pedal—which greatly alters the characteristic sound of the trumpet.

Listen to Miles Davis' "Black Satin" (1972).

The title track of "On the Corner" features a dense groove that is very much like a James Brown groove in its complexity, even though the sound and detail of the "On the Corner" groove is not exactly straight-ahead funk. About 2:30 into the piece, the drummers shift to a more rock-based beat. "On the Corner" features an extended soprano sax solo by Dave Lieberman who was brought into the session after it had begun and could barely hear what the other musicians in the session were doing! The piece ends abruptly, since it segues into the next piece on the album, "New York Girl," which we will not listen to here.

Mahavishnu Orchestra (1971-75)

In 1970, McLaughlin became a disciple of Indian guru Sri Chinmoy. At this time he also started the Mahavishnu Orchestra (1971), a name suggested by Chinmoy, that featured a fusion of jazz, rock, and Indian music.

McLaughlin's jazz improvisation with the Mahavishnu Orchestra was very rock-oriented and featured his blistering, rapid-fire, and many-notes-per-second guitar virtuosity. He also frequently altered his amplified sound along similar lines as other contemporary rock guitarists.< /p>

There have been three incarnations of the Mahavishnu Orchestra, the last in the mid-1980s, the second which featured the electric violinist John Luc Ponty, and the first and most talented version of the band which featured McLaughlin on electric guitar,

Jerry Goodman on violin, Rick Laird on electric bass, Billy Cobham on drums, and Jan Hammer on piano and synthesizer. Read about Jan Hammer. Billy Cobham, who also played with McLaughlin in Miles' bands from 1969-71, was a strong influence in the Mahavishnu Orchestra. Read about Billy Cobham.

The three pieces of "The Sunlit Path," "La Mere de la Mer" [The Sea of the Sea], and "Tomorrow's Story Not the Same" are played as a single, three-part work in a live, 1972 performance released the next year as Between Nothingness & Eternity (1973).

Herbie Hancock

Herbie Hancock

Review, if you would like to, the article on Herbie Hancock.

Along with his groundbreaking work with drummer Tony Williams and bassist Ron Carter with Miles Davis, Herbie Hancock also recorded many early and important fusion works. For example, his hit "Watermelon Man" (1962), featured a strong gospel sound. Throughout the 1960s, Hancock was also composing music for television and radio commercials, so he knew what was popular and what would sell.

"Cantaloupe Island" is a classic example of his early 1960s fusion work from the album Empyrian Isles (1964) that featured Hancock (keyboards), Freddy Hubbard (trumpet), Ron Carter (bass), and Tony Williams (drums).

Chick Corea

Another former member of Miles' bands (1968-70) is keyboard great Chick Corea who, along with Miles alumni Herbie Hancock and John Coltrane Quartet pianist McCoy Tyner, are probably the most influential post-1960s keyboard players in jazz. Read about Chick Corea.

Corea has created and continues to perform with some of the most important fusion groups working in jazz. Among many, including Circle (1969-72), is Return to Forever (1971-72) that created an interesting mix of jazz, Latin, and pop music. From 1973 into the late 1970s, an electric version of Return to Forever was fronted by Corea, now performing on Fender-Rhodes electric piano, Hohner Clavinet, Yamaha Organ, and Minimoog and ARP Odyssey synthesizers. The band also featured, in turn, on electric guitar Bill Connors, Earl Klugh, and virtuoso guitarist Al Di Meola, drummer Lenny White (another former Miles band member), and electric bass wonder Stanley Clarke. Read about Stanley Clarke.

"Vulcan Worlds" is a Stanley Clark composition that features unison ensemble playing that was heavily synthesized and full of electronics, all set over rock beats and funk bass lines. Listen to Return to Forever's "Vulcan Worlds."

In the late 1980s, Corea put together another virtuosic fusion group called the Elektric Band, which consisted of

Chick Corea, midi piano and synthesizers

Eric Marienthal, sax

Frank Gamble, guitar

John Patitucci, electric bass

Dave Weckl, drums

They released two very successful recordings, Elektric Band (1986) and Inside Out (1990) and further spawned an acoustic trio—Akoustic Band—of Corea (acoustic piano), John Patitucci (acoustic bass), and Dave Weckl (drums) drawn from the larger, electric ensemble.

"Inside Out" is the title track of the 1990 Eleckric Band release. It is a very commercially-appealing, fusion work. However, most of the chords used in the piece are major 7th chords and the piece also has a great deal of parallel motion. "Inside Out" opens with an interesting chord progression whose roots ascend in an alternating whole and half step pattern (am7/D-bm7/D-CM7/D-dm7/D-Eb = whole-half-whole- half steps), that is then followed by a root movement that descends byhalf steps (in mm. 10-11, we hear CM7-BM7-BbM7-A7-AbM7-Gm7).

Listen to Chick Corea and Elektric Band's "Inside Out" (1990).

 

Miles in the 1980s

Miles in the 1980s

Miles returned to performing after a stroke in 1982 and moved into a much more aggressive club fusion style. True to form, he was able to identify and hire many young and innovative musicians into his band, including Branford Marsalis (tenor and soprano sax), John Scofield (electric guitar), Bob Berg (tenor sax), and Bill Evans (tenor and soprano sax). He often used multiple guitarist, many of whom were very rock-oriented.

Miles also continue to exploit African imagery and contemporary sounds in his late 1980s albums. His 1986 release, Tutu, features dominant synthesizer work by Marcus Miller (with whom he had worked previously in 1983). Miller played most of the instruments on the album as the songs were built up in the studio, much like a pop recording—instrument by instrument—creating a heavy, club beat for many of the cuts.

"When I talked to [Miles] about the forties and the fifties," said Marcus Miller, "I got the impression that there was a direct link between the way people walked down the street and the popular music and the bebop that these guys played. The bebop was just an extension of the dance music of the time. So the music was connected with everything in Miles' life, and when you realize that, you begin to see that for a guy like him, music is just a reinterpretation of what's going on. And that's all it is. So for him it didn't make any sense to play in 1983 the same music he'd played in 1963. I learned that you should connect your music with your times. The downside being that the music might not last, because there's been plenty of music that was connected to its time and just stayed there. You can only hope that at a certain point it will transcend its time. But you can't think about that: you have to concentrate on making your music relevant to what's going on" (Szwed 2002, 384).

Jazz critics initially attacked Tutu, much like they had earlier with Bitches Brew and for many of the same reasons. However, Warner Bros. promoted the album heavily, created a music video for it by the young, unknown, film director Spike Lee, and Tutu eventually won a Grammy.

In "Splatch," Miles improvs over a heavy club beat. The piece features Miles on trumpet backed by Marcus Miller's synthesizer work. Adam Holzman is also featured on a synthesizer solo and Paulhino da Costa and Steve Reid add extra percussion.

Listen to Miles Davis' "Splatch" (1986).

"Perfect Way" from Tutu features an even more commercial groove by Marcus Miller that closely duplicates the original by Scritti Politti over which Miles improvises (Szwed 2002, 383).

Listen to Miles Davis' "Perfect Way" (1986).
