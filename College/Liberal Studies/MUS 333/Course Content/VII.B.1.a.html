Coleman Hawkins

Coleman Hawkins was an enormously influential tenor sax player and an important figure that help transform swing into bebop. One recording that helps cast into sharp relief the differences between swing and bebop improvisation is Coleman Hawkins' landmark 1939 recording of Johnny Green's popular song "Body and Soul."

First, though, read the article on Coleman Hawkins.

Let's start off with a classic swing version of "Body and Soul," recorded in 1935 by swing clarinet great Benny Goodman backed by Teddy Wilson on piano and drummer Gene Krupa. We will discuss Benny Goodman's career later in the Swing section of the course.

In Goodman's recording of "Body and Soul," he presents a notable example of the common swing manner of improvising in a popular song ballad (a moderately-paced love song). Goodman keeps the melody audible virtually throughout his improvisation. The melody is decorated or merely ornamented. He adds turning figures as decoration and modifies the rhythm of the original only enough that a listener (or dancer) would never loose the sense of the original tune. In effect, the melody is stylized rather than recomposed.

The form of "Body and Soul" is 32-bar A A B A form (song form). Please listen closely to just the first chorus performed by Goodman in the first two A sections, then Wilson on piano in the bridge (B), with Goodman returning for the final A section.

Coleman Hawkins, after leaving swing great Fletcher Henderson's big band in 1934, he worked with a number of European groups (and American musicians working in Europe like saxophonist Benny Carter) for five years. In 1939, Hawkins was back in the US and back in the recording studio as a bandleader of a newly-formed nonet (nine piece) band.

Hawkins' 1939 recording of "Body and Soul" could not be more different than Benny Goodman's recording made just four years earlier. Rather than focusing on the tune like Goodman, Hawkins soon casts off much of the tune and creates a wholly new composed (improvised) line in an even eighth note rhythm. He uses melodically or linearly spread out chords tones (called an arpeggio) that harmonize the tune. Hawkins' improvisation is generated whole cloth from the chord changes and completely turns away from swing's signature thematic or decoratively melodic focus. By the second chorus, Hawkins has left the melody and never turns back.
