Introduction

To quote jazz columnist Joe Goldberg,

"Jazz musicians were probably the first people to use the word "cool" who weren't talking about the weather." (Gene Sculatti, The Catalog of Cool, Warner Books. 1982.)

The two prevailing ideas concerning the evolution of cool jazz in the late 1940's are:

  1. The most popular idea:

    As a reaction to the frantic tempos and chord-laden music of bop â€“ i.e. "Hot Jazz". Cool jazz was far more subtle and restrained than bop. While cool jazz and bop were both popular jazz styles that existed at the same time, the proponents of one style were often loath to admit that the other even existed. Cool jazz players were considered wimpy and "white" by boppers who themselves were, in turn, thought of by the cool jazz players as crazy and out of control. These cool jazz players didn't drop everything bop however. The cool jazz players developed the advanced harmony (scales & chords) put forth by the boppers even further than the boppers did.

  2. A less popular idea:

    As a natural evolution of the more relaxed, genteel playing of jazz musicians like tenor saxophonist Lester Young. Young's light saxophone sound coupled with the unhurried pace of his solos contrasted sharply with the "in your face" sounds and virtuosic solos characteristic of bop playing. (Bop will be covered in detail in the next section of the course,) Listen to Young's solo from the 1939 recording of "Lester Leaps In" that he played with Count Basie on piano. The recording was made almost 10 years before there was any mention of a style called cool jazz. Listen to a minute or two to hear one of the original "cool" musicians.

While some jazz writers may feel that one of these ideas may supersede the other, both of them contributed equally to the creation of cool jazz. By the way, journalists looking for a name for this laid back jazz style coined the term "cool jazz" not the musicians who played it.

In general, cool jazz, like the name implies takes an easier to digest, softer less edgy and aggressive approach to jazz music. The music often has an intellectual, almost classical music sound to it, which can elicit a feeling of emotional detachment. Oddly enough, in spite of this description, cool jazz was a big hit with audiences and its influence is still being felt today. People still ask bands to play Dave Brubeck's Take Five. Today's "smooth Jazz" while not as rich harmonically or compositionally as cool jazz was is successful for the same softer, non-aggressive reasons.
