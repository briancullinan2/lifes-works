New Orleans (1900-1917)

New Orleans had historically been one of the major ports of the African slave trade. It was an international city, with strong French roots. By the early decades of the twentieth century, there were Italian, French, and English opera houses and a thriving African American musical community. The breadth of African American music in New Orleans was huge, from African American middle class musicians and musicians playing for the middle class—with its emphasis on European art music—to the many bands that attracted patrons for the bars, gambling houses, dancehalls, and houses of prostitution in poorer quarters. The latter places were concentrated in a thirty-eight square block area of New Orleans noted for its legalized gambling and prostitution. Called Storyville after the city councilman who established the red-light district, this was New Orleans ’ main tourist attraction as people steamed down the Mississippi to the city.
