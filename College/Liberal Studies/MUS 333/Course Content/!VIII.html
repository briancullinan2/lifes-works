Swing musical style

While influenced by the Blues, especially in a style of Swing that developed in Kansas City, Swing jazz was primarily organized through the popular songs that were set in arrangements for the new big bands.

Big bands are easy to spot—they truly are big. These ensembles of ten or more musicians marked a change from the small group jazz from earlier times and from what would follow in Bebop and other modern jazz styles.

Big bands are built of sections, or groups, of like instruments from the brass and woodwind families, coupled with a rhythm section.

Brass section of a big band usually had two to five trumpets (with three being the standard number) and one to five trombones (with two to three being standard).

The sax, or reed section of the big band usually found three to five musicians musicians, playing the following instruments (this distribution of five saxes was standard).

two alto saxes

two tenor saxes

one baritone sax

Some big bands added a clarinet to sound the highest reed voice.

The big band rhythm section usually employed the piano, rhythm guitar, bass, and drums.

Piano

The piano played chords (called ‘comping’) and also played melodic solos.

Rhythm guitar

The rhythm guitar replaced the banjo from earlier jazz ensembles. It, like the banjo, easily projected in the ensemble when playing in a loud, strumming rhythm. The rhythm guitar did not play single line melodies, which would have to wait for the rise of the amplified, or electric guitar. While the rhythm guitar played chords, like a piano, its function was also to articulate a steady rhythm like the drummer.

Acoustic bass

The acoustic or string bass outlined chord roots in a style of playing called a ‘walking’ bass line, where the performer improvised a bass line that primarily moved by step to the next closest note in the scale (called conjunct melodic motion), rather than skipping around like earlier jazz bass lines that were more like the ‘oompah’ lines that you can hear tubas play in bands (called disjunct melodic motion). In fact earlier jazz, like Dixieland and Ragtime (the latter which we already discussed) commonly used tubas, rather than the acoustic bass, which only replaced the tuba during the 1930s.

While the bass player often served as a time-keeper, like the drummer, in many big bands bass lines became more melodic over time.

Drums

The swing drummer’s primary job was to keep the beat steady (remember that this is dance music) and often emphasized the ‘back beat’ (or beats two and four in a duple meter of four beats). Like the bass player, Swing drummers rarely had the opportunity to solo. The drummer principally would be featured in fills when the ensemble was not playing.

Most importantly, the rhythm section as a whole helped create a swing feeling in the big band.

 

The ‘swing’ in Swing jazz

The ‘swing’ in Swing jazz

Swing jazz has a distinct feel that carried through into Bebop and still is cultivated by most contemporary performers. Some modern jazz performs, like Cecil Taylor, dropped the swing feel developed during the 1930s, but most jazz performers still maintain a vestige of swing feeling in their music.

Swing makes the music move, jump, and pop. It was a great style to use to accompany dancing and one to sell to audiences and listeners either in clubs, through radio broadcasts, or on recordings. Swing makes the music active and very kinetic.

Big band arrangements

Big band arrangements

Since big bands were so large, it was not possible to informally organize a performance like one could do in a Bebop combo. Earlier styles of jazz, like Dixieland, featured all performers improvising solo lines simultaneously and later jazz styles, like Bebop, featured a solo improviser backed by a rhythm section (which was also improvising).

Also, with bigger groups, personnel would come and go. It was easier to have most of the big band reading an arrangement, rather than teaching new musicians (who would need to memorize it) what the band played.

Arrangers (either the band leader, or someone hired within the big band or outside of it) would write arrangements for the big band. The arrangements were usually based on a popular song of the day. There were defined sections for soloists, which many musicians found constricting, and melodies were written out for the whole band to play either in unison or in harmony.

Introduction

Introduction

From the early 1930s to the later 1940s, Swing jazz dominated the American popular music scene and—Swing was dance music! Swing featured a great dance feel: moderate tempos, a ‘swing’ feeling that was very kinetic and active, popular songs arranged for big bands that were recognizable and an attraction in itself. Swing also benefited from the increasing sophistication of music technology (like the spread of radio and the development of electronic microphones and recording technology) and the reduction in price of means of acquiring music (record players, records, and radios all were dropping in price).

Swing was music that people purchased, consumed, and used for diversion. This was the time of the Great Depression through the Second World War. It was a time of terrific displacement. During the Depression, many African Americans and white Southerners moved to cities in the North, Midwest , and West looking for work. They took their music with them. Their Southern, rural music changed as they experience new, tougher conditions in the cities. Rural music and lyrics now became transformed into edgier, urban styles.

In jazz, music evolved into a more commercial style that crossed racial lines and appealed to majority and minority listeners. Swing was the popular music of its day. Swing is what you would have heard in clubs, on the radio, and hummed by almost everyone. Swing represents the high water mark for jazz’s popularity. Never before, nor since, has jazz been America’s main form of popular music like it was with Swing.

Don Redman

After leading bands in the New York area for a few years, Henderson took a band into the popular Roseland Ballroom in 1924. Together with his arranger and alto saxophonist Don Redman, he created music that set the standard for jazz big bands from the late 1920’s on and became one of the most widely imitated.

Read the article on Don Redman

Both Duke Ellington and Benny Goodman, whose bands would dominate the 1930s, were influenced by the Henderson Orchestra. Henderson hired the best jazz musicians available among them: Louis Armstrong (trumpet), Coleman Hawkins (tenor sax) and Rex Stewart (cornet).

Henderson and Redman took the trumpet, trombone, and clarinet front line of the earlier Dixieland ensemble and made each instrument the leader of four or five-person trumpet, trombone, and reed (clarinet and saxophone) section. With a three or four-piece rhythm section, the instrumentation of the big band was established by Henderson and is the standard today.

Redman, perhaps in cahoots with Henderson, devised a format for arranging that revolutionized the big band sound, while capturing the polyphonic (many solo lines sounded at the same time) New Orleans style of ensemble playing. It’s known as a ‘block passage’ or ‘block voicing.’

In a block passage or voicing the melody line is played by the lead instrument of a section. The other members of the section are each assigned a note of the chords that is used to harmonize the melody. The rhythm of all of the parts is the same as the melody, so all members of the section play the same rhythmic pattern.

“The Stampede”

New York Swing

“The Stampede”

“The Stampede,” recorded by the Henderson Orchestra in 1926, is a transitional work that contains elements of both Dixieland and Swing styles. The song was composed by Henderson and arranged by Redman. The rhythm section, with the inclusion of the tuba and banjo, is pure Dixieland. The tuba is playing on beats 1 and 3 of each bar throughout the entire performance, while the banjo accents beats 2 and 4. The resulting two-beat feel is standard for Dixieland. The horn background heard during the tenor saxophone solo is played on beat 2 of each bar, further establishing the two-beat feel.

Aside from the tenor solo background, the treatment of the horns is quite different from that of a Dixieland band horn section. The evolution toward Swing is clear in a number of ways.

  • There is no group improvisation. Only one instrument is featured in the solo sections.
  • The solos themselves are more lyrical. The improvised lines are much longer and are more melodic in character.
  • The horns not playing a solo are used strictly as accompaniment
  • There are distinct divisions between the band ensemble sections and the solos. An ensemble section ends and a solo section begins—a solo section ends and an ensemble section begins.
  • Both Don Redman as the arranger and his written arrangement have taken on greater importance. The arrangement is not improvised. Individual parts are provided for and are read by every player.

“The Stampede” is an ABAC form and features solos by trumpet players Rex Stuart and Joe Smith. Coleman Hawkins, one of the few players who made the transition from Dixieland through Swing to Bop, plays the tenor saxophone solo.

“Wrappin’ It Up”

New York Swing

“Wrappin’ It Up”

Redman left the Henderson Orchestra in 1927 to become musical director for the McKinley’s Cotton Pickers. Under his influence, the band was transformed into a precise, swinging group. He went on to lead his own band from 1931 to 1940, which was considered one of the top bands of the period. In 1940, he began to work as a freelance arranger, creating music for Paul Whiteman, Count Basie, Jimmy Dorsey and many others.

Redman had written virtually all of the arrangements played by the Henderson Orchestra from 1924 to 1927. With Redman gone, Fletcher Henderson became the chief arranger for his band. His composition and arrangement of “Wrappin’ It Up” was recorded just over eight years after “The Stampede” in 1934. It’s hard to believe that both pieces were recorded by the same band! The Swing era in full bloom! The two-beat rhythm feel of “The Stampede” has been replaced by a steady four-to-the-bar rhythm feel. The tuba has been replaced by a string bass and the banjo has been replaced by a guitar. While the band is larger by only one player, the arrangement written by Henderson has the different sections of the group—saxes, brass (trumpets & trombones) and the rhythm section—are sounding separately from one another. “Wrappin’ It Up” is an ABAC form.

Jimmie Lunceford

New York Swing

Jimmie Lunceford

Born in 1902 in Fulton , Missouri and educated in Denver , Colorado where he earned a Bachelor of Arts degree at Fisk University , James Melvin Lunceford organized a band in the late 1920s while an athletic instructor at Manassas High School in Memphis , Tennessee . It developed into one of the finest and most popular bands of the swing era. At first called the Chickasaw Syncopators, the Jimmie Lunceford Orchestra was considered by many as the equal of both the Basie and Ellington bands.

Read the article on Jimmie Lunceford

The disciplined, precise section playing was of the Lunceford band was unrivalled. Known more for its ensemble playing rather than its soloists, the Lunceford Orchestra developed through the arrangements of trumpeter Sy Oliver and others a distinctive two-beat swing that became known during the Swing era as the ‘Lunceford two-beat.’

The Jimmie Lunceford Orchestra followed the Duke Ellington Orchestra into New York’s Cotton Club in 1933. With its combination of superb musicianship and clever arrangements of the popular songs of the day, the popularity of the Lunceford group blossomed. Inventive novelty, comedy and vaudeville style acts endeared Lunceford to a wide audience. The shows included costumes, skits and vocals performed by band members. Oliver became one of the band's star singers, as did trombonist Trummy Young, who had a hit with his recording of “‘Tain't What You Do.” Despite the band’s comic side, Lunceford always maintained a high degree of professionalism when it came to performing his music.

 

Glen Miller

New York Swing

Glen Miller

Glenn Miller led one of the most popular dance bands of the day. Influenced by Jimmie Lunceford, Glenn rehearsed his band to heights of musical perfection. A fine trombonist and arranger, Miller’s popularity grew as his performances were broadcast live on the NBC radio network. His singers included Ray Eberle and Marion Hutton, the sister of movie star, Betty Hutton. The Miller band also featured a vocal group called The Modernaires.

The distinctive Miller sound featured a clarinet lead rather than an alto sax playing melodies in the sax section. Miller hits that included “Moonlight Serenade” (composed and arranged by Miller), “ Pennsylvania 6-5000” (a novelty tune that had the band members singing) and “In the Mood” are still being played today. Miller led a band in the Army Air Corps during World War II where he was killed when his plane disappeared over the English Channel in 1944.

 

Tommy Dorsey

New York Swing

Tommy Dorsey

Tommy Dorsey and his brother Jimmy, after breaking up their shared band in 1935 in a disagreement over a tempo, led two extremely popular bands during the Swing era. Tommy Dorsey made his name as the ‘Sentimental Gentleman’ of Swing, featuring a heavy vibrato on his trombone that set the standard for sweet bands to come. His theme song and biggest hit was “I'm Getting Sentimental Over You.” In the 1940s, the singer in his band was none other than Frank Sinatra. Dorsey also featured a vocal group called the Pied Pipers.

Paul Whiteman

Paul Whiteman, named by press agents the ‘King of Jazz,’ put together his orchestra in the 1920s. Alvino Rey and Larry Clinton

While Miller, the Dorsey brothers, and Whitman included some good improvised, ‘hot’ jazz in their performances, bandleaders Alvino Rey and Larry Clinton catered to the dancers. Along with bandleaders Vincent Lopez, Hal Kemp and Kay Kyser, the music of Rey and Clinton fit firmly into the sweet or society band mold. This music is anything but ‘hot!’

Larry Clinton first made it in the music business as an arranger for many of the top bands in the 1930s. He worked at various times for Tommy and Jimmy Dorsey and the Casa Loma Orchestra (a band that was considered a cooperative band, as there has no leader).

Society Orchestras (Sweet Bands) and Commercial Swing

New York Swing

Society Orchestras (Sweet Bands) and Commercial Swing

The Swing era was not only ushered in the big band as we know it today but also the Depression. After the stock market crashed in 1929, the majority of Americans fell on hard times. Jobs were scarce, money was tight and the bread line was born. People needed a diversion, something to take their minds off of the precarious economic situation they found themselves in. For many, going to a dance hall and dancing to the music of a big band was the ticket. Bandleaders were happy to oblige and the Society or Sweet bands (bands that played primarily for dancing) flourished.

Prohibition ended in 1933 and people were ready to have a good time. When the band started playing, couples got up from their tables and danced. The Lindy Hop (aka the Jitterbug) was danced to fast or ‘hot’ songs. The Fox Trot was the dance of choice for slower or ‘sweet’ songs. Most if not all of these bands featured a ‘boy’ or a ‘girl’ singer(s) or a vocal group that crooned the popular songs of the day. Society bands were light on improvised solos—‘solos’ were often written out and played the same way every performance—and heavy on ensemble playing.

 

New York Swing: Black and White

New York Swing

Introduction

In the late 1920s, New York City became the jazz center of the United States. Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington and many other jazz innovators lived and performed there. Music publishing companies and recording studios proliferated. ‘Tin Pan Alley’ churned out songs capable of greater harmonic sophistication and evolution. Radio networks were based in New York and broadcasted big band music around the country. Musicians migrated to the ‘Big Apple’ from all over the country including New Orleans and Chicago bringing with them their experience in not only jazz bands, but also brass and military bands, vaudeville bands, and dance bands. The result was a cross-fertilization between educated musicians, music readers, and non-music reading improvisers. The jazz music that was composed and played in this melting pot of styles came to be known as New York Swing.

 

Tailoring arrangements to his sidemen

Duke Ellington

Tailoring arrangements to his sidemen

Ellington was a master at writing arrangements for his big band that always emphasized the strengths of his sidemen (musicians playing the his big band).

By 1927 Ellington had some of his all-star musicians already in place in his group and he featured their talents in his tailor-made arrangements. His big band would grow to twelve musicians and included the likes of Bubber Miley and then Cootie Williams on trumpet, Joe ‘Tricky Sam’ Nanton on trombone, Johnny Hodges on alto sax, and the great baritone saxophonist Harry Carney. Their recordings of “East St. Louis Toodle-Oo” and “Black and Tan Fantasy” showcase their talents.

 

The Cotton Club’s ‘Jungle Music’

Duke Ellington

The Cotton Club’s ‘Jungle Music’

From 1927 to 1931 Ellington and his group were featured at the Cotton Club in Harlem where they played so-called ‘jungle music’ numbers that featured many faux African musical stereotypes: deep sounding drums, ‘growling’ brass effects (a specialty of trumpeter Bubber Miley), exotic-sounding harmonies, and melodies based on pentatonic (five pitch) and whole tone scales. Duke Ellington and his Famous Orchestra’s 1927 recording of “East St. Louis Toddle-Oo” exemplifies this then-popular style.

“East St. Louis Toddle-Oo” is roughly in A A B A form with new material added in a C section. This C section would be moved around by Ellington in recordings of other arrangements of this tune throughout the 1920s and it even disappears in recordings after 1937.

“Mood Indigo”

Duke Ellington

“Mood Indigo”

Moreso than his ‘jungle’ period works, it was the widespread popularity of his 1930 recording of “Mood Indigo” that made Ellington an international star. Originally recorded as “Dreamy Blues,” it was soon renamed “Mood Indigo.” It was based on the popular song written by Ellington and clarinetist Barney Bigard with words by Irving Mills.

In “Mood Indigo,” you hear solos by Joe ‘Tricky Sam’ Nanton on trombone, Ellington on piano, Artie Whetsol on trumpet, Barney Bigard on clarinet, and Nanton on trombone to close.

Ellington’s arrangement was breathtakingly original. He turned the traditional scoring in a big band on its head! What is usually found—clarinet played in the high register with trumpet sounding the melody accompanied by trombone in a lower register sounding a counter melody—is inverted so that in “Mood Indigo” we hear a muted trumpet on top along with a plunger-muted trombone in its high-register played in harmony with the trumpet, while the clarinet is sounding more than an octave below the trumpet and trombone.

Listen to Duke Ellington and His Cotton Club Orchestra’s “Mood Indigo” (1930).

 

“Cotton Tail” / “Hot Chocolate”

Duke Ellington

“Cotton Tail” / “Hot Chocolate”

Ellington’s big band settled into its mature instrumentation in the 1930s of six brass, four reeds, and four in the rhythm section. By 1939, a couple of important new musicians joined the band, including Ben Webster on tenor sax, Jimmy Blanton on bass, and Billy Strayhorn soon-to-be Ellington’s chief collaborator in composing and arranging.

From the early 1930s through the early 1940s, Ellington continued to write increasingly sophisticated arrangements for his group. This was the time when he wrote the popular song “I Got It Bad” and the great compositions and arrangements for his big band such as “Concerto for Cootie,” “In a Mellotone,” “Blue Serge,” and “Cotton Tail.”

“Cotton Tail,” which we have listened to earlier in the course, also was called “Hot Chocolate” by Ellington and was the subject of a feature film short on the band from 1941. “Blue Serge”

Duke Ellington

“Blue Serge”

Another example, by what is probably one of the greatest Swing big bands ever, was the 1941 recording of “Blue Serge.” The arrangement is of a slowly-paced, four-beat-to-the bar, eight (8) bar blues (rather than the more common 12 bar blues). With each chorus lasting only eight bars, things could get repetitive and stale. Ellington avoids this trap by adding a couple of bars to some of the choruses (even adding four bars to Ben Webster’s solo chorus), thus keeping audiences on their toes.

In “Blue Serge,” Ellington also connects the 3rd, 4th, and 5th choruses together so that they flow as if they were one long formal unit. Ellington sunders the form by leaving out the cadence (or resolution) at the end of the 3rd chorus and starting his piano solo before the 4th chorus ends (rather than waiting until the beginning of the 5th chorus).

Additionally, “Blue Serge” is not based upon a popular song like so many of his other famous arrangements. It is much more of an ‘instrumental’ composition. For example, the opening trumpet melody features difficult intervals and unexpected harmonic changes that moves over a wide range. We see Ellington in “Blue Serge” moving beyond what a Swing dance arrangement was commonly thought to be. In the hands of late 1930s through 1940s Ellington, Swing jazz was becoming music for listening as well as dancing. Jazz is now becoming a form of art music that will become full blown in the hands of Bebop performers.

Benny Moten

Kansas City Swing

Benny Moten

After being stranded in Kansas City in 1927, Basie stayed on and became an influential member of both bassist Walter Page’s Blue Devils band and then Benny Moten’s Kansas City Orchestra in 1929.

Read the article Bennie Moten

Benny Moten’s Kansas City Orchestra’s 1932 recording of “Moten Swing” is a good example of something part-way between Fletcher Henderson’s New York Swing style and the more mature Kansas City Swing style that we will continue to discuss. “Moten Swing” is not based upon 12-bar blues, but rather on popular song form (A A B A). The chordal, rather than melodic structure of the arrangement is also more New York (a feature of Walter Donaldson’s arrangements for the Guy Lombardo and his Royal Canadians’ sweet band) than Kansas City. The original melody of “You’re Driving Me Crazy” (the chords of which were borrowed for “Moten Swing”) sounds only in the second A section of the second chorus.

The style-defining Kansas City riff rhythm is still present and serves to bring things to a climax. We also hear Count Basie play in several different pianos styles, from the New York stride style of James P. Johnson and Fats Waller of the first chorus, ‘boom-chuck’ Dixieland-style, and boogie-woogie in the bridge of the second chorus. It is, however, at the very end of the piece that we hear the style that he made famous: spare, full of suggestion, and simple.

Basie and his rhythm section

Kansas City Swing

Basie and his rhythm section

The rhythm section was the core of any Kansas City Swing big band and Basie’s rhythm section was one of the very best in jazz. It was anchored by Basie on piano, Walter Page on bass, Jo Jones on drums, and (after 1937) rhythm guitarist Freddie Green. They did not simply play a static pattern, but moved in response to either a soloist or section work. Drummer Jo Jones moved his pulse into the high-hat cymbals from the bass drum and changed Swing’s sound.

Basie’s rhythm section also helped establish the four beat swing of the era that supplanted the two-beat style of earlier jazz and some New York Swing styles. Bassist Walter Page fully outlined the bass line to the extent that pianist Basie no longer had to reinforce it and was free to focus on other things. They were one of the most rhythmically tight rhythm sections in the business.

 

“Doggin’ Around”

Kansas City Swing

“Doggin’ Around”

“Doggin’ Around” was recorded by Count Basie and his Orchestra in 1938, just six years later than “Moten Swing,” but this band is much more sophisticated and virtuosic. You’ll also hear the legendary Basie rhythm section. The sound and feel that these four men created became and remains the Basie band’s signature. No other band of any size or period of jazz music elicits such instant recognition.

The melody of “Doggin’ Around” was written and arranged by Edgar Battle and tenor saxophonist Herschel Evans. It’s in a 32-bar A A B A song form. As you listen, notice that the B section has no written melody, only chords. The B section was always improvised. Listen carefully and you’ll hear that the background riffs played during the solos in choruses 2, 3 and 5 are different for each soloist. No background riffs are played in section B during any of the solos.

The 5th Chorus also features a solo by the great Lester Young. Notice how different his tenor sax solo sounds than Herschel Evans’ solo in the 2nd Chorus. They were as contrasting tenor sax players as one could find. Lester Young was this cool, restrained, and consummately elegant player sitting, unruffled, amid the explosion that was the Basie band. As discussed earlier in the course, Young would have a seminal influence on the development of Cool jazz.

Kansas City Swing

Kansas City Swing

Introduction

At the same time Lunceford, Redman, Ellington and the society bandleaders were crafting arrangements for their bands and featured soloists, something different was going on in Kansas City, Missouri. In contrast to the complex arrangements of the eastern bands, the Kansas City brand of swing music evolved around what became known as ‘head’ arrangements. A head arrangement is a type of arrangement developed and fine-tuned by the players in the band during a performance and one that is not written down by one person - the arranger. As a result, the players (aka sideman) in a Kansas City band had a much greater impact on the sound of the band than their eastern counterparts. Because of the looser compositional format, the focal point of the music turned to the soloist and away from the arranger. The success of a Kansas City band was dependent upon the strength of its players.

The twelve-bar blues form and the A A B A song form with blues-tinged chord progressions were the most popular vehicles for improvisation. Kansas City jazz musicians developed riff-based arrangements based on simple, short syncopated ideas that were played in unison by the section voicing the riff. These riffs were played behind both the melody and the solos. New riffs were composed spontaneously as the inspiration struck the players. Many of the riffs were so popular that they became songs themselves! Count Basie’s theme song, “One O’clock Jump,” started out as a riff.

From the late 1920s to the late 1930s Kansas City was run by Tom Pendergast, an alderman, who opened the city to liquor, gambling, and prostitution. This, in combination with the end of Prohibition and the Depression, turned Kansas City into a freewheeling, no-holds-barred town that supported over 300 nightclubs.

 

Big band and combo

Benny Goodman

Big band and combo

Much of Goodman’s time in New York was spent working for leaders who functioned more as personalities than performers. Goodman believed that musicians, not entertainers, should build a band. Forming his own band in 1934, Goodman at first patterned his group after Glen Gray and his Casa Loma Orchestra, a jazz-oriented band popular with college audiences at the time. Soon after, Benny and his band were playing on the three-hour coast-to-coast radio program Let’s Dance alongside Kel Murray’s dance band and Xavier Cugat’s Latin band for a twenty-six week run.

At the start the band was basically a dance orchestra and only occasionally played a ‘hot’ tune or two. For this engagement, Goodman brought in arranger Fletcher Henderson to spice things up. Benny was not a fan of the music being played by the sweet bands and wanted to present music that was ‘hotter.’ The public, however, wasn’t ready for what the band had to offer. A nationwide tour followed Goodman’s engagement at the Roosevelt Hotel in New York and both were dismal failures. All of that changed when the Goodman band performed at the Palomar Ballroom in Los Angeles. Whitney Balliett, jazz columnist for the New Yorker Magazine described it best.

In 1935, the clarinetist and bandleader Benny Goodman, aged just twenty-six, left New York with his fourteen-piece "swing" band and, traveling in a rag-tag group of cars, headed for the huge Palomar Ballroom in Los Angeles. It was not an easy trip. There were half a dozen dismal, sparsely attended one-nighters and three weeks at a dance hall in Denver, where the band was forced to play waltzes, tangos, and novelty numbers. On the opening night at the Palomar, the band played ballad numbers in the first set, and there was little response from the dancers. Then one of the musicians said, if they were going to bomb again they might as well do it in style. So Goodman called for his hot, often up-tempo arrangements, many of them by the ingenious black bandleader and arranger Fletcher Henderson, and the kids stopped dancing, clustered around the bandstand, and began roaring. Before the weeks at the Palomar were over, it was clear that Goodman had suddenly made jazz—still a suspect and largely subliminal American folk music, despite the brilliant inventions during the previous decade of Jelly Roll Morton, Louis Armstrong, Sidney Bechet, and Duke Ellington—into a popular music (Balliett 2000).

The radio broadcasts from the Palomar were heard all over the United States. The success of Goodman and his band was assured. When Benny brought his band to the Paramount Ballroom in New York later that year, people were literally dancing in the aisles. His performances were sold out wherever he went. Swing music had taken the world of music by storm and Goodman led the way.

Benny’s virtuosity on the clarinet was unmatched. No one could play with such flawless technique and with such a beautiful sound. He surrounded himself with the best musicians he could find and gave no thought to their race. Goodman’s groups always maintained the highest standards of musicianship. His bands and combos were among the first to have black and white performers playing together in public.

Benny took great pleasure in featuring his sideman as soloists. Drummer Gene Krupa, pianist Teddy Wilson, and trumpet players Harry James and Ziggy Elman went on to form their own bands as a result of the exposure Goodman gave them. The fans adored them. Watch and listen to Benny and the band perform “Let me Off Uptown.” The all-star cast of this video includes vocalist Anita O’Day, trumpet player Roy Eldridge, and drummer Gene Krupa.

Charlie Christian

Benny Goodman

Charlie Christian

African-American Charlie Christian gave birth to modern jazz guitar. Christian advanced the guitar beyond the confines of the rhythm section as the first guitarist to explore the instrument’s solo potential. He played an amplified electric guitar in a style derived from Lester Young’s cool, uncluttered saxophone solos. Born in Dallas in 1916, Christian and his family moved to Oklahoma City , where he began playing the guitar at age twelve, advancing to professional gigs by the time he turned fifteen.

Read the article on Charlie Christian

Record producer John Hammond brought Charlie Christian and his superlative guitar playing to Benny Goodman’s attention in the summer of 1939. After hearing Charlie play, Goodman hired him on the spot as a member of the Benny Goodman Sextet. Christian was a featured soloist in the Goodman band for two years until his untimely death from tuberculosis in 1942 at just age twenty-five. In spite of his short career, Christian became the most influential guitarist jazz has ever known and his influence is still being heard today in the playing of John Abercrombie and Pat Metheny.

In the 1941 sextet recording of “I Found a New Baby,” you’ll hear Christian at his best. His choice of notes (harmonies) and rhythmic phrasing suggests the beginnings of Bop. The Goodman Sextet was fully integrated, containing three white players – Goodman, George Auld on tenor sax, and Artie Bernstein on bass – and three African-American players – Christian, Count Basie on piano, and Jo Jones on drums.

Lionel Hampton

Benny Goodman

Lionel Hampton

Lionel Hampton, born in 1909 in Louisville, Kentucky, started his career as a professional musician as a drummer. At a 1930 recording session, Louis Armstrong asked Lionel, or ‘ Hamp’ as he was known in the music business, if he could play behind him (Louis) on “Memories of You” and “Shine” on the vibraphone that was sitting in the studio. Hamp became the first musician, jazz or otherwise, to record an improvised solo on the vibraphone. Hampton was a featured soloist in quartets and quintets led by clarinetist Benny Goodman in the late 1930s. Forming his own big band in 1940, Hampton had a huge hit with his recording of “Flyin’ Home.” His music remains popular with Swing music enthusiasts.

Read the article on Lionel Hampton

In the following 1939 recording of “When Lights Are Low,” you’ll hear Hamp playing in the midst of one of the many all-star groups that he assembled over the years. Soon-to-be Bop pioneer Dizzy Gillespie is playing trumpet; Benny Carter (the composer and arranger of the song and a top swing musician) is playing the alto sax; and Coleman Hawkins, Ben Webster and Chu Berry are all playing tenor sax. The rhythm section has Clyde Hart on piano, Charlie Christian on guitar, Milt Hinton on bass, and Cozy Cole on drums. Hampton’ s fluid technique and infectious swing are clearly evident in this recording.

Benny Goodman

Benny Goodman

Introduction

Benny Goodman—who came to be known as ‘The King of Swing’—was born in 1909, the son of poor Russian immigrant parents. A child prodigy, he took up the clarinet at age ten and by age fourteen was playing professionally. Goodman joined Ben Pollack’s band in 1926. His roommate while with Pollack was Glenn Miller, and both Tommy and Jimmy Dorsey played in the same band. Goodman left Pollack in 1929 and became a successful studio musician working in New York City.

Read the article on Benny Goodman

However, it is as a bandleader that Goodman made history. As a musician who demanded only the best, he racially integrated his bands and combos in his search for the best players jazz had to offer. His music defined the wing era and he became a national icon as popular as Elvis Presley and the Beatles from later decades.
