Course description
This course examines the historical development of jazz through its economic, political, social, and artistic (cultural) contexts. The course will specifically address how and why people historically construct, socially maintain, and individually create and experience this music.

The thematic focus of the course will be to Value the Diversity of Human Experience. It is a liberal studies course in the Cultural Understanding distribution block. The course will address the essential skills of critical thinking and critical reading and will pay particular attention to them through assessment.

There will be a variety of levels of reading that students critically engage throughout the course including the course text written by the instructors, secondary journal and reference articles written for the profession, and primary source readings. In course Exams and the Written Assignments, critical thinking and critical reading will be demonstrated through engagement with the above activities.

Student learning outcomes
Successful students in this course should be able to discuss and demonstrate for assessment how and why people historically construct, socially maintain, and individually create and experience jazz (valuing the human experience, critical reading, critical thinking).

Successful students in this course should be able to demonstrate online collaboration skills, including teambuilding, online project management, communication via the Internet, group decision making, conflict management, and interpersonal skills.

Paper topic
Please read the interview from a 1985 issue of Musician magazine with jazz greats Herbie Hancock and Wynton Marsalis, titled “Soul, Craft, and Cultural Heirarchy” by editor Robert Walser.

As you read through the interview, you will find an increasing divergence in fundamental assumptions between Hancock and Marsalis. BE WARNED—you will encounter strong language, imagery, and ideas with which you may agree or disagree. Please also read through the brief introduction to the article written by Rob Walser, the Chair of the Ethnomusicology Department at the University of California at Los Angeles, which puts the article and the two musicians into context. At root, the issues that Hancock and Marsalis discuss can be applied to earlier jazz as well—just the particulars would change. Of the many ideas discussed between Hancock and Marsalis, select one idea that you find interesting on one side or the other of the argument to be used as the focus of your paper. Please back up your assertions with course materials and/or your own research. As you begin your paper, you may want to focus on the jazz you have studied in the course to date. However, you may want to enlarge you focus in a later draft to include other jazz styles. That will be up to you.

You must cite references to any materials drawn from beyond this course (you do not need to cite course materials). Use any citation system that you are familiar with (Chicago Manual, MLA, etc.).  Be consistent, though, in whatever system you do choose. Although based on your reading of the assigned article, good papers usually focus on one aspect of your overall interaction and thought process. You need to actively shape material from the article: that is, be strong and clear in your statements about and insights into the arguments presented in the article, then utilize the arguments presented in the article to help you develop your ideas. Focus and develop your discussion; do not just recount the article. Remember that there are many ways to discuss the issues raised in the article; therefore, you must be sure to explain and detail how you see things and why. Do not feel as if you need to discuss the entire article to write a good paper. Be careful of simply summarizing issues in the article. Be explicit and explanatory.

Paper grading rubric
Below is the rubric that will be used to assess each of your Outline, Draft, and Final Papers.

Clarity of purpose. topic, and theme (25% of overall grade for each Draft or Final Paper)
It should be clear to your reader (your TA) specifically what you are discussing. Analytical writing should be explicit and focused. Be wary of having only a general sense of what you want to discuss or of developing two different themes in your paper. Some sentence early in the paper should offer your major, overriding insight (your Thesis Statement)—the point around which the whole paper adheres.

Substantiation (25%)
There should be a clear and definite sense of how the ideas in the paper originated in and developed from your interaction with the specifics of the article under consideration. This requirement helps you clarify your insights and helps someone else, like your TA, see your perspective more clearly. Substantiation can take the form of referring to specific details, conflicts, resolutions, images, and/or using quotations to verify your points.

Coherence (20%)
All sections/paragraphs of your paper should help develop the topic/theme. Coherence means that there exists a unity of purpose and focus throughout the paper; that you do not meander to some other interest and that you do not lose track of the specific theme of your paper.

Cohesiveness (20%)
There should also be a sense of connection between paragraphs, some feeling of transition from one paragraph to the next—a developmental progression that builds throughout the paper. Some relationship should exist between the paragraphs in terms of how they forward or continue the discussion of your theme/topic.

Grammar/language/expression (10%)
The way in which you form your sentences, your use of the English language, and the way you express your ideas are also taken into consideration.
