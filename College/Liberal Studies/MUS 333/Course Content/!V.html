Introduction

To quote jazz columnist Joe Goldberg,

"Jazz musicians were probably the first people to use the word "cool" who weren't talking about the weather." (Gene Sculatti, The Catalog of Cool, Warner Books. 1982.)

The two prevailing ideas concerning the evolution of cool jazz in the late 1940's are:

  1. The most popular idea:

    As a reaction to the frantic tempos and chord-laden music of bop â€“ i.e. "Hot Jazz". Cool jazz was far more subtle and restrained than bop. While cool jazz and bop were both popular jazz styles that existed at the same time, the proponents of one style were often loath to admit that the other even existed. Cool jazz players were considered wimpy and "white" by boppers who themselves were, in turn, thought of by the cool jazz players as crazy and out of control. These cool jazz players didn't drop everything bop however. The cool jazz players developed the advanced harmony (scales & chords) put forth by the boppers even further than the boppers did.

  2. A less popular idea:

    As a natural evolution of the more relaxed, genteel playing of jazz musicians like tenor saxophonist Lester Young. Young's light saxophone sound coupled with the unhurried pace of his solos contrasted sharply with the "in your face" sounds and virtuosic solos characteristic of bop playing. (Bop will be covered in detail in the next section of the course,) Listen to Young's solo from the 1939 recording of "Lester Leaps In" that he played with Count Basie on piano. The recording was made almost 10 years before there was any mention of a style called cool jazz. Listen to a minute or two to hear one of the original "cool" musicians.

While some jazz writers may feel that one of these ideas may supersede the other, both of them contributed equally to the creation of cool jazz. By the way, journalists looking for a name for this laid back jazz style coined the term "cool jazz" not the musicians who played it.

In general, cool jazz, like the name implies takes an easier to digest, softer less edgy and aggressive approach to jazz music. The music often has an intellectual, almost classical music sound to it, which can elicit a feeling of emotional detachment. Oddly enough, in spite of this description, cool jazz was a big hit with audiences and its influence is still being felt today. People still ask bands to play Dave Brubeck's Take Five. Today's "smooth Jazz" while not as rich harmonically or compositionally as cool jazz was is successful for the same softer, non-aggressive reasons. Gerry Mulligan

Gerry Mulligan

Next to Harry Carney, Duke Ellington's longtime baritone saxophone player, Gerry Mulligan was probably the best known and highly regarded baritone saxophone player in modern jazz. In addition to his remarkable abilities as a player, Mulligan was also a fine arranger, composer and bandleader who led a number of groups that helped to define the cool jazz movement.

Mulligan's first big break came when Gene Krupa hired him as both saxophonist and arranger in the 1940's. His bop influenced writing caught the attention of big band leader Claude Thornhill. It was in Thornhill's band that Mulligan met Gill Evans. This serendipitous meeting helped to lay the groundwork for Miles Davis' Birth of the Cool recording. Before the Birth of the Cool nonet played its engagement at the Royal Roost, Mulligan, Gil Evans, Miles and number of other musicians interested in something new were gathering at Evan's Manhattan apartment to play, arrange and refine the nonet's sound. Mulligan's contribution to the group and subsequent recording included arrangements of Darn That Dream and Godchild. He also composed and arranged three original songs Venus de Milo, Rocker and Jeru. Mulligan's use of mixed meters and phrases of varying lengths helped to create an arrangement full of unexpected twists and turns. Mulligan moved to Los Angeles after the Birth of the Cool recording sessions and helped to establish what became known as West Coast Jazz. The label is a misnomer as most of the people involved with West Coast Jazz were not from the west coast! Gerry Mulligan, for example, was born in Queens . New York â€“ almost as far from the west coast as you can get and still be in the United States ! The label came to signify music played by white musicians in a very relaxed, laid back style totally ignoring the contributions being made by African-American musicians like tenor saxophonists Dexter Gordon and Buddy Collette who were performing in the LA area at the same time.

Before returning to New York in the late 1950's Mulligan and trumpeter Chet Baker formed a group with no piano in it. This piano-less quartet (sax, trumpet bass and drums) created quite a sensation and was recorded often by the recording company, Pacific Jazz. The lack of a piano left a lot space for the soloists to fill by themselves. They were now free to experiment with the chords and scales (harmony) and not be bound by the pianist. Because of this new found freedom, Mulligan and Baker were able to weave spontaneously created melody lines around each other. This counterpoint became a feature of cool jazz. Let's listen to Mulligan and Baker along with Carson Smith on bass and Larry Bunker on drums in a 1953 live recording of My Funny Valentine. An A-A-B-A form, the last letter A section is 12 bars long â€“ 8 bars plus a 4 bar tag. The performance is two choruses long and is anchored by what seems to a written out (not improvised) bass line. The line is repeated with very few alterations for both the trumpet and baritone sax solos.

Miles Davis

Miles Davis, who has been discussed in the Cut 'n' Mix, Fusion and Modal & Free Jazz sections of this course, was also one of the most important contributors to the cool jazz style. During the mid '40's, he played and recorded with bop saxophonist Charlie Parker. While still playing and recording with Parker and feeling the need for a change of direction in his music, Miles put a 9-piece band (a nonet) together in the late '40's with another of cool jazz's important contributors, composer/arranger Gil Evans. Their idea was to come up with music that embraced more refined, cerebral sensibilities that were not present in the freewheeling, highly charged bop style.

Evans, baritone saxophonist Gerry Mulligan, pianist John Lewis and John Carisi wrote the arrangements. The often lush harmonies, orchestral colors and contrapuntal lines (two or more melodies being played at the same time) found in the arrangements gave the impression of a small big band rather than that of a big small band or combo. The arrangements themselves took on as much importance as the solos creating almost seamless transitions from ensemble sections where all of the horns were playing to a solo and back again to the ensemble.

The nonet played its only live engagement for two weeks at the Roost in 1948. In 1949-50 the group recorded 11 songs at the Capitol Records Studio in New York . First released in the early '50's on 78 rpm records (which may account for the short length of the performances â€“ 78's could only handle 3-4 minute of music per side), all 11 of the songs were released in 1957 on a 12" LP entitled The Birth of the Cool. The live engagement and the recording constituted the entire output of the band!

While not a big seller at the time, its effect on jazz musicians was profound. Cool jazz had matured into a viable style that others, especially on the West Coast, began to explore and are still exploring today.

Let's listen to a cut from The Birth of the Cool. "Boplicity", written by Miles Davis and arranged by Gil Evans. Evans was a master of combining advanced harmonies with a splendid ear for orchestration. The addition of a french horn and a tuba, instruments not usually found modern jazz bands, to the lineup of players and Evans' arrangement with its flowing lines creates a peaceful, relaxed feeling â€”i.e. Cool. The solos are low-keyed and devoid of fast, technical passages. The song is in an A-A-B-A form, each section 8 measures long.

Before leaving "Boplicity" let's take another look at the printed music. You are probably wondering why punctuation marks and mathematical symbols appear in the music. What do dashes, dots and commas have to do with music making? Why do musicians pay attention to "greater than" (>) symbols and to "is greater than or equal to" (â‰¥) symbols? To musicians playing any style of music, these punctuation marks and math symbols have nothing to do with either English or Mathematics. These dashes, dots, and commas, are called "articulation marks" and tell the player how to play the note that one of them or a combination of them is attached to.

In measure 1 you'll se a dash above the third note. It's commonly called a "long mark" and it tells the player to play the note full value, in this case a quarter note (two eighth notes tied together). There is no audible space between it and the note that follows every time you see a dash â€“ the notes are connected to one another.

The note that follows has a dot underneath it. The dot or "staccato mark" means that the note is played shorter than is printed â€“ often half of its value. Here the quarter note sounds like an eighth note â€“ half of a quarter is an eighth. Listen for a short but audible space between it and the next note every time you see a dot.

Take a look at measure 4 and find the comma above the staff before the last note. The comma is called a "breath mark." For every one in the band it means that the note just before the breath mark (comma) is to be played a bit shorter so that there is an audible space between it and the next note. (Probably for the wind instrument players (saxes, trumpets and trombones) it's a good place to take a breath, too!) The breath mark differs from the dot in that the note it follows is note played half of its value but just a tad shorter. As you listen to the recording listen for the space between the notes separated by a breath mark.

Measure 6 has the first of the math symbols in it â€“ the greater than symbol (>). To the musician this symbol is called an "accent mark." A note with an accent mark placed above or below it is given a little more emphasis, a slight increase in volume.

The math symbol over the last note in measure 17 (â‰¥) is the "is greater than or equal to" symbol. To the musician it's a combination of two articulation marks â€“ in this case an accent mark we just discussed along with a long mark talked about earlier. So we have a note that is emphasized and played full value.

Now go back and listen to the track one more time and pay particular attention to the articulation marks.

The Miles Davis and Gil Evans collaborations continued on well into the '50's with the 1957 release of Miles Ahead, 1958 release of Porgy and Bess and the 1959 release of Sketches of Spain.

Evans arranged the music from George Gershwin's Broadway show Porgy and Bess for Miles and big band. The arrangement of the song "Summertime" continues the cool feeling established with The Birth of the Cool recordings. Again you'll hear lush harmonies and orchestral colors heard in the earlier recording this time on a larger scale as the band playing behind Miles is much larger â€“ twenty players compared to nine. Evans added French horns, a tuba and alto flutes to the saxes, trumpets and trombones normally found in a big band creating unusual, yet engaging sonorities.

After a straightforward, unadorned performance of the familiar melody, Davis plays three solo choruses accompanied by a light, unobtrusive band background. Miles is playing with a harmon mute throughout the recording.

Lennie Tristano

Lennie Tristano

Lennie Tristano (piano), Lee Konitz (alto sax)' Billy Bauer (guitar); Arnold Fishkin (bass); Shelley Manne (drums)

Pianist Lennie Tristano and alto sax player Lee Konitz (of The Birth of the Cool recording fame) were two players who, unfortunately, received little or no recognition from the general public. They were considered "musician's musicians" and plied their craft in and around New York City.

Tristano's music featured unusual, often complex melodies and harmonies played with a rhythm section (bass & drums) than was relegated to a subdued, strict time keeping role, quite different from the often flamboyant bop rhythm sections of the day. To quote Martin Schowten in his notes to the Capitol LP "Crosscurrents", "Lennie's [Tristano] select coterie of musicians [Konitz (alto sax), Warne Marsh (tenor sax), and Billy Bauer (guitar)] developed a low-temperature [read "cool"] approach to composition and improvisationâ€¦ In terms of the dynamic level, nothing much was allowed to happen â€¦"

Tristano, blind from infancy, was also a prominent jazz educator whose teachings influenced many, many players in the early 50's, among them, Lee Konitz. Konitz came to prominence playing with Claude Thornhill's band in 1947 the same year he joined Miles Davis, Gil Evans Gerry Mulligan in the project that created The Birth of the Cool recordings. All of this while he was just 20 years old! He became a teacher to students all over the world and has won many jazz polls as best alto player.

"Subconscious Lee" is a Lee Konitz composition based on the chord progression of Cole Porter's "What Is This Thing Called Love," and has an AABA form, each section 8 measures long. (It was and is common practice for jazz composers to borrow a chord progression that he or she likes and to write a new melody over it.) Take a look at the melody and notice that in every A section the melody is different but the chords remain the same. While the solos in this particular recording aren't quite as "low-temperature" as Mr. Schowten suggests, they do, however, possess less drive, rhythmic variety and emotional content then their Bop counterparts.

Dave Brubeck Quartet

The Dave Brubeck Quartet was a very popular group on college campuses during the 50's and into the '60's. His recordings Jazz Goes to College and Time Out were big hits. Brubeck and his group â€“ Paul Desmond (alto sax), Eugene Wright (bass), Joe Morello (drums) â€“ actually made recordings on college campuses often to the chagrin to some college administrators who thought jazz music was only appropriate for parties and not for either recordings or the concert stage.

A student of French classical composer Darius Milhaud at Mills College in the 40's, Brubeck brought much of what he learned to jazz. His compositions are filled with counterpoint, harmonies and forms that show the influence of his European teacher. You heard a composition of his The Duke in the discussion of Miles Davis and Gil Evans. What is thought of to be Brubeck's most famous song, Take Five, was not written by him but by alto saxophonist. Paul Desmond. Desmond was the personification of the cool jazz sax player. His airy sax sound coupled with lyrical solos is being copied today.

Take Five is one of the most popular jazz songs ever written. The title refers to the fact that the song is played in a 5/4 meter, five beats to the bar, not common at all with jazz players. The famous melody is announced by an equally famous bass line. I've played the song many times and can tell you that as soon as the audience hears the bass part, the applause starts as the wait for the melody to begin.
