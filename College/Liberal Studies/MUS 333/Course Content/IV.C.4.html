Late Coltrane

As we move into the mid-1960s, John Coltrane began to explore increasingly radical and emotional means of musical expression.  He was working on the edge of the jazz avant-garde.  However, unlike many other 1960s experimenters, he continued to attract a large following.

Pianist McCoy Tyner left Coltrane and his quartet in 1966 and was replaced by Alice McLeod (Coltrane), his second wife.  He also added a second tenor sax player to the group, the innovative Pharaoh Sanders, as well as a second drummer, Rashied Ali, and several African-influenced percussionists.  He would, at times, feature up to three tenor saxes, two alto saxes, and two basses in his group, in addition to those mentioned above.

In Coltrane’s late work (1965-67), his interest in tonal organization and even playing specific pitches waned.  He and Sanders began to exploit and use radical timbres that more resembled human cries.  Textures became increasingly dense and full of sustained dissonances all moving within a loose structure.  He and his group pursued collective improvisation that emphasized texture and register (highest range of the sax).

At the same time, his later work also became much more overtly spiritual and meditative in albums such as Om (1965), Mediaitions (1965), and Intersellar Space (1967).

"Mars" from Interstellar Space was recorded shortly before his death in 1967.  This is a duet album, with only Coltrane on sax (and bells) accompanied by Rashied Ali on drums.  Coltrane "always played off the drums," said Rashied Ali.  "He told me the drums freed him, he didn’t have to worry about chord changes.  He got the freedom to play what he wanted from working directly off the drums" (Thomas [n.d.],202).
