Introduction

There were several swing performers that laid the foundation for later developments in bebop and cool. Tenor saxophonists Coleman Hawkins, Ben Webster, and Benny Carter and trumpeter Roy Eldridge changed the way swing musicians improvised. Pianist Art Tatum brought an incredible virtuosity to the table that bebop would seize upon and continue to develop. Tenor saxophonist Lester Young would lay the groundwork for a new, cooler style of expression.

Coleman Hawkins

Coleman Hawkins

Coleman Hawkins was an enormously influential tenor sax player and an important figure that help transform swing into bebop. One recording that helps cast into sharp relief the differences between swing and bebop improvisation is Coleman Hawkins' landmark 1939 recording of Johnny Green's popular song "Body and Soul."

First, though, read the article on Coleman Hawkins.

Let's start off with a classic swing version of "Body and Soul," recorded in 1935 by swing clarinet great Benny Goodman backed by Teddy Wilson on piano and drummer Gene Krupa. We will discuss Benny Goodman's career later in the Swing section of the course.

In Goodman's recording of "Body and Soul," he presents a notable example of the common swing manner of improvising in a popular song ballad (a moderately-paced love song). Goodman keeps the melody audible virtually throughout his improvisation. The melody is decorated or merely ornamented. He adds turning figures as decoration and modifies the rhythm of the original only enough that a listener (or dancer) would never loose the sense of the original tune. In effect, the melody is stylized rather than recomposed.

The form of "Body and Soul" is 32-bar A A B A form (song form). Please listen closely to just the first chorus performed by Goodman in the first two A sections, then Wilson on piano in the bridge (B), with Goodman returning for the final A section.

Coleman Hawkins, after leaving swing great Fletcher Henderson's big band in 1934, he worked with a number of European groups (and American musicians working in Europe like saxophonist Benny Carter) for five years. In 1939, Hawkins was back in the US and back in the recording studio as a bandleader of a newly-formed nonet (nine piece) band.

Hawkins' 1939 recording of "Body and Soul" could not be more different than Benny Goodman's recording made just four years earlier. Rather than focusing on the tune like Goodman, Hawkins soon casts off much of the tune and creates a wholly new composed (improvised) line in an even eighth note rhythm. He uses melodically or linearly spread out chords tones (called an arpeggio) that harmonize the tune. Hawkins' improvisation is generated whole cloth from the chord changes and completely turns away from swing's signature thematic or decoratively melodic focus. By the second chorus, Hawkins has left the melody and never turns back.

Ben Webster

Ben Webster

Ben Webster, along with Coleman Hawkins and Lester Young, are probably the most influential of all tenor sax performers. As an improviser, Webster was always very tuneful in his approach so that his improvisation sounds nearly like a complete thought.

Read the article on Ben Webster.

In 1940, Duke Ellington arranged for his big band a new tune, "Cotton Tail," composed by Webster to the chord changes of George Gershwin's popular song "I Got Rhythm." The Gershwin song and its harmony dictate the form of the new piece: 32-bar A A B A song form. Note that Ellington drops the final A section of the first chorus and substitutes a four bar transition to the second chorus.

Listen, especially, to the second and third chorus which features Webster. Composer Gunther Schuller comments that Webster often liked to enter and exit a solo section "unobtrusively, flowing in, as it were, on the prevailing tide of the music. His stealthy entrance on Cotton Tail is in its subtleness and surprising understatedness one of the all-time hair-raising moments in jazz" (Schuller 1989,528). "And Ben! Oh that Ben!" said Ellington big band guitarist Fred Guy, "You could feel the tension rise the moment he began to solo" (Lawrence 2001,291).

Note, too, Ellington's very forward-looking arrangement in the fifth chorus going into the first half of the sixth chorus which anticipates more modern styles of improvisation, here written out for the full sax section of his big band.

Trends that would influence bebop

 

Virtuosity

Read the article on Art Tatum.

Art Tatum possessed one of the most remarkable virtuosic talents in jazz. Classical (art music) pianists regularly attended his concerts. Watch a performance of Art Tatum.

Tatum’s 1949 recorded version of Ann Ronnell’s popular song, “Willow Weep for Me,” is a breathtaking show piece. He takes repetitive sections of the song as a license for further reworking, yet helping create an almost hypnotic effect. The piece opens with a 4 bar vamp, whose time is pushed and pulled (an expressive technique called ‘rubato’ by musicians). Tatum also alters the character of each chorus which allows him to continue to vary the piece, yet still retain a larger sense of formal coherence. In “ Willow, Weep for Me” the first chorus has a very George Gershwin sound, composer of many poplar songs, American in Paris, and Rhapsody in Blue. The second chorus, in contrast, emphasizes the blues underpinning of the original song. Tatum’s performance follows the 32 bar song form of the original, with the final A section of the second chorus also serving as a double-time coda.

Trends that would influence cool

Trends that would influence cool

There were other musicians working in swing that would profoundly shape the development of cool. Probably the single most influential swing musician for cool has to be tenor sax great, Lester Young.

Lester Young

Read the article on Lester Young.

Amid the screaming energy of Count Basie’s Kansas City style swing big band, Lester Young sat in the middle and barely broke a sweat. He put the ‘c’ in cool.
