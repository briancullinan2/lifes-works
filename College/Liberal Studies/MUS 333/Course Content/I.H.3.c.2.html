String instruments

Banjo

The banjo is an African-American string instrument with frets that is a New World modification of a West African instrument. The banjo was very popular in nineteenth-century American and was used in early jazz, country, and roots music. The banjo features a drum-head resonator and four to five strings. The instrument really puts out quite a bit of sound. Early twentieth-century jazz banjo players strummed chords in a 'claw hammer' style, while the popular bluegrass three to five finger 'rolls' were not popularized until later in the century by Earl Scruggs.

Acoustic guitar (aka Guitar)

The guitar supplanted the banjo in the 1920s-30s in jazz and other popular music styles, such as the blues. Guitars were cheaper than banjos, were mass-produced, and marketed and sold through many retail outlets like the mail-order Sears Catalog. The guitar also had a mellower sound than the banjo, was played with a pick, had six or twelve strings, and was fretted.

Watch and listen to guitar virtuoso and Gypsy Jazz great Django Reinhardt perform "Le Jazz Hot." Watch and listen to the example.

Electric guitar (aka Arch-tops)

Electric guitars used in jazz are usually hollow-bodied, like an acoustic guitar, but with an arched-top and pick-ups that transfer the sound to an amplifier. The hollow-bodied electrics have a sound that is closer to the acoustic guitar, than the solid bodied instruments that became popular in Rock at the end of the 1950s.
