Big band arrangements

Since big bands were so large, it was not possible to informally organize a performance like one could do in a Bebop combo. Earlier styles of jazz, like Dixieland, featured all performers improvising solo lines simultaneously and later jazz styles, like Bebop, featured a solo improviser backed by a rhythm section (which was also improvising).

Also, with bigger groups, personnel would come and go. It was easier to have most of the big band reading an arrangement, rather than teaching new musicians (who would need to memorize it) what the band played.

Arrangers (either the band leader, or someone hired within the big band or outside of it) would write arrangements for the big band. The arrangements were usually based on a popular song of the day. There were defined sections for soloists, which many musicians found constricting, and melodies were written out for the whole band to play either in unison or in harmony.

Listen again to Duke Ellington and His Famous Orchestra’s “Cotton Tail” (1940). The first chorus features the melody played in unison by saxes and trumpets.

1st chorus (Melody)

0:00-0:09 A 8mm saxes and trumpets play tune

0:09-0:16 A 8mm

0:16-0:25 B 8mm sax and trumpets in call-and-response pattern

0:25-0:29 transition 4mm no final A

After the melody was stated, a chorus that featured a solo improviser backed by the rhythm section or full ensemble usually followed.
