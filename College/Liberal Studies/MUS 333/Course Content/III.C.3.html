On the Corner (1972)

In his 1972 album On the Corner, named after the corner that a young Miles could see from his home behind his father's dentist office in East St. Louis, Miles said that "I was trying to play the music that I grew up on now, that roadhouse, honky-tonk, funky thing that people used to dance to on Friday and Saturday nights" (Szwed 2002, 325). John Szwed comments that Miles wanted to "thicken the groove by filling in the space where harmonic variation would have been in conventional jazz" (Szwed 2002, 326).

The album was panned by critics and even fellow musicians. Stan Getz ripped the album by saying that the "music is worthless. It means nothing. There is no form, no content, and it barely swings. The soloists are playing a half tone above and a half tone below so it'll sound modern, but there's nothing to build on or anything logical—nothing" (Milkowski 1993, 6). Getz and other critics were behind the curve and it took almost a generation for jazz musicians to catch up with what Miles was doing on this album.

In On the Corner, Miles was pushing things even further than on Bitches Brew. The funk grooves are more pronounced and Miles is starting to use a wah-wah pedal (usually found in a guitarist's set up) on his amplified trumpet. The mix can be quite dense and he used a large number of musicians on the two days of recording sessions. At this time, Miles was obsessively listening to Sly and the Family Stone's "In Time" and the album was heavily influenced by it and the music of James Brown (funk)—of whom Miles was a great fan—rock guitarist Jimi Hendrix, and avant-garde art music composer Karlheinz Stockhausen.

We are going to listen to two cuts from On the Corner: "Black Satin" and the title track "On the Corner." On June 1, 1972 Miles used the following musicians to record, among other works, "On the Corner." A number of the musicians were either regular or former members of Miles bands (*appears before their names).

Miles Davis, amplified trumpet with wah-wah pedal

*Dave Liebman, soprano sax

*Chick Corea, synthesizer

*Herbie Hancock, organ

Harold I. Williams, electric piano

*John McLaughlin, electric guitar

*Michael Henderson, electric bass

*Jack DeJohnette, drums

Billy Hart, drums and percussion

*Don Alias, conga and percussion

James 'Mtume' Forman, hand drums and percussion

Electric bassist Michael Henderson was a former member of Stevie Wonder and Aretha Franklin's bands and specialized in a Motown groove bass style.

Several non-standard jazz instruments were also used on the session, including eelctric cello (played by Paul Buckmaster), sitar (Collin Walcott), and tabla (Badal Roy). Sitar and tabla are both classical north Indian instruments and were popular sounds in late 1960s-70s recordings due to the use of them by the Beatles. Sitarist Walcott was also a member of the jazz-world music fusion group Oregon that featured oboe player Paul McCandless as the group's leader.

On June 2, 1972 Miles used many of the same musicians, but substituted the following for some from the June 1 session. This is the session where "Black Satin" was recorded.

*Carlos Garnette, alto and tenor sax (replaces Liebman)

*Bennie Maupin, bass clarinet

Lonnie Liston Smith, organ (Hancock moves over to electric piano and synthesizer)

David Cramer, guitar (replaces McLaughlin)

In "Black Satin," after an Intro by tabla, sitar, and synthesizer (they also close the piece with an Outro), the groove kicks in and you can hear both a bass vamp and a strong James Brown influence on the recording since Miles' drummer lifted his lick from James Brown's recording of "Cold Sweat" (Milkowski 1993, 7).Miles' trumpet—which plays the melody once the groove kicks in—is both amplified and run through a wah-wah pedal—which greatly alters the characteristic sound of the trumpet.

Listen to Miles Davis' "Black Satin" (1972).

The title track of "On the Corner" features a dense groove that is very much like a James Brown groove in its complexity, even though the sound and detail of the "On the Corner" groove is not exactly straight-ahead funk. About 2:30 into the piece, the drummers shift to a more rock-based beat. "On the Corner" features an extended soprano sax solo by Dave Lieberman who was brought into the session after it had begun and could barely hear what the other musicians in the session were doing! The piece ends abruptly, since it segues into the next piece on the album, "New York Girl," which we will not listen to here.
