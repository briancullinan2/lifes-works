Introduction

The first of a series of self-proclaimed modern jazz styles began to coalesce in the mid-1940s. It was called bebop or bop, some saying that its name was culled from vocal jazz improvisations called scat. Bebop is "a most inadequate word," wrote novelist Ralph Ellison, that "throws up its hands in clownish self-deprecation before all the complexity of sound and rhythm and self-assertive passion which it pretends to name" (Ellison 1966,202 quoted in DeVeaux 1997,1).

Bebop embodied a very self-conscious turning away from the popular music roots of earlier jazz styles. Bebop improvisation would also become the benchmark for modern jazz that many performers still use to evaluate one another. Yet, bebop still retained the forms and standard songs of earlier jazz. The virtuosity that was highly prized by earlier jazz was honed to a level never thought possible. These elements really did keep bebop closer to what came before.

So, bebop balances many contradictions, tensions, and conceits. On a larger level, we will explore the crashing together of artistic creation, bebop, social, economic, political, and racial issues in the post-World War II years in the United States later in the Topics section of the course. For now, we will explore what makes bebop sound different than what came before or after it. Bebop and modal: style characteristics

Bebop and modal: style characteristics

Bebop paved the way for the first truly modern style of jazz, modal, which tossed aside long-standing practices of improvising over a series of chord changes within a form shaped by popular music standards. Modal slowed the progression of chords down to a single complex scale. The rapid-fire, many notes-per-second virtuosity that we find in bebop was put aside by some influential modal musicians, like Miles Davis.

Yet, bebop and modal performances were built around the same small group combo of rhythm section (bass, drums, piano) and solo instrument(s). However, the differences between bebop’s small combo and the big band from the style of jazz that preceded it, swing, are quite dramatic.

Bebop and swing: style characteristics

Bebop and swing: style characteristics

The swing big band, as we will discuss in more detail in the next section of the course, was quite large with up to twenty musicians performing together mostly reading pre-written arrangements that allowed little opportunity for solo improvisation. While there were a few great bebop big bands, like those lead by Billy Eckstein and Dizzy Gillespie, most of the action in bebop occurred within a quartet or quintet.

Bebop was ‘ear’ music, or music that was created to be listened to (like art music). Its tempos were often very fast and the rhythmic motion was frequently doubled so that either the rate that the chords and melody went by or the amount of rhythmic activity per second dramatically increased. Swing and earlier jazz was dance music that used moderate tempos that were good for dancing.

The rhythm section in swing outlined a four-beat pattern that was very stable and danceable. Bebop’s rhythm section created stacked layers of syncopation that created polyrhythms and polymeters, or the effect of multiple rhythms or meters played simultaneously.

Even different instruments were emphasized in the two style periods. Benny Goodman’s swing clarinet virtually disappeared in bebop (the bass clarinet would surface in some modal recording played most prominently by Eric Dolphy, but the clarinet has never come back to the level of prominence that it experienced in swing). In bebop, there is a focus on saxes and trumpet as solo instruments, with the electric guitar also gaining attention.

As mentioned before, swing musicians had little opportunity for solo improvisation in the complex arrangements played by swing big bands, while bebop combo performances were built exclusively around improvisation with a ‘tune’ sounded at the top and close of a piece that was often simply voiced by the soloists in unison.

Virtuosic display was a hallmark of bebop improvisation that was writ by being able to play technically challenging and rapid figures that were generated from many chromatic chord substitutions to an original chord progression in difficult or non-standard keys. Bebop improvisers also used a number of musical ideas per solo break. Both later and earlier jazz styles usually focused on a single idea or two to develop into a solo passage.

Swing improvisation tended to be more like embellishing a popular song melody. This was a smart move for commercial music that was dependent upon consumers recognizing the original hit melody and following it throughout the improvisation. Bebop performers completely discarded the melody after an initial statement. This process gives rise to the modern concept of improvisation as truly composing and performing in the moment.

Even the type of tunes that were composed and used in performance differed between swing and bebop. Swing melodies were well-known (or soon to be) popular hit songs of the day that were usually simple and easy to understand at first listening (again, this was a smart strategy for music that was meant to be sold as a commodity). Bebop melodies, however, were much more complex, convoluted, and full of contrasts and surprises.

The harmonies used as chord sources for improvisation or as the basis for the arrangements in a swing big band piece were also relatively simple compared to what would be used by bebop musicians. Swing harmony, with the exception of the likes of Duke Ellington, was straight-forward material generated from the pitches of a particular diatonic scale. Chord progressions were usually simple triads (three note chords built from the pitches of a scale), seventh chords (four note chords), some augmented or diminished chords (chords that are stacks of all major thirds or minor thirds), and the occasional added sixth chord (C-E-G-Ab, or scale degrees 1-3-5-lowered 6, where the Ab is added to a C major triad as a lowered sixth scale degree in C major).

Bebop harmonies were much more complex and chromatic (introducing pitches from other scales), than swing harmony. Bebop harmonies extended traditional, swing-based harmonies by using more complex chords by stacking additional thirds to chords (creating ninth, eleventh, thirteenth chords) that were frequently also populated by chromatic borrowings from other scales. Further, bebop musicians often substituted more complex chords for simpler ones found in standard popular song chord progressions.

Swing used closed phrasing, where the melody, improvisation, or chord progressions all articulated or closed (cadenced) together in lockstep. With bebop, things were much more ambiguous and musicians cultivated a more open-ended and unresolved quality. There was a sense of whimsy to many bebop performances that were full of twists and turns and unexpected surprises.

Bebop and swing: rhythm sections

Bebop and swing: rhythm sections

A similar gulf existed between bebop and swing when one looks at how the rhythm section accompanied soloists. Swing rhythm sections played static and predictable patterns, so that people could still dance while a soloist improvised. In contrast, bebop rhythm section accompaniment followed the ebb and flow of the soloist’s improvisation to a very high degree. There was a sense of a dialogue between the soloist and the rhythm section. For example, swing pianists comped in swing or stride style that was regular and predictable, while bebop pianists injected commentary into the mix.

The swing drummer kept time on the high-hat cymbal which was reinforced by the snare and bass drums. The bebop drummer put the time in the suspended cymbal, while developing polyrhythmic layers on the other drums.

Overall, the differences between swing and bebop come down to the fact that swing was familiar, popular, commercial music that was comfortable and accessible for audiences. It was dance music. Bebop was much more agitated and consciously intellectual music.

Charlie Parker

Charlie Parker

Charlie Parker was one of the most important and influential musicians in the history of jazz. His approach and characteristics of his style of improvisation became the basic language of bebop and was adopted by his fellow musicians. Parker was revered by his contemporaries and still casts a long shadow among jazz musicians.

We will return to Charlie Parker in Topic 4 later in the course and discuss some of the more technical sections of an article that you are about to read. For now, make sure that you have a general grasp of Parker's career and his relationship to the foundation of bebop. Read the article on Charlie Parker.

The year 1945 was important for Parker. He returned to the recording studio, after the long American Federation of Musicians strike, this time as a bandleader. Parker also began to work regularly with bebop trumpet great Dizzy Gillespie. It is a time when bebop in full bloom. Fast and furious! Lots of chords!

Parker's 1945 classic recording of "Koko" features Parker on alto sax, Dizzy Gillespie on trumpet and piano, bassist Curly Russell, and Max Roach on drums. "Koko" is based on the chord progression from the 1938 Ray Noble song "Cherokee," a favorite vehicle for bebop improvisers. The form is song form in a pattern of A A B A. The tempo is as fast as you could want it, with the quarter notes moving at 308 beats per minute. As a result of this blistering pace, Parker and Gillespie double the time usually spent in each of the form's sections so that each A A B A section is sixteen bars long, rather than the more conventional eight.

The song is played in Bb major and the chord progression is rather difficult to negotiate, especially with four key changes in the B or middle section (called the bridge).

B section or Bridge

Measures 1-4 of the bridge is a 2-5-1 progression in B major (moving from chords built on the second (2) to the fifth (5) to the first (1) scale degrees in B major)

Measures 5-8 is a 2-5-1 progression in A major

Measures 9-12 is a 2-5-1 progression in G major

Measures 13-16 is a 2-5-1 progression in F major

While all of these modulations may seem easy to improvise over, bear in mind that the song is usually played at an unbelievably fast tempo, as mentioned above. The true test of a bebop improviser at a jam or cutting session was to see who could play through the progression the fastest and in any number of keys! Parker and Gillespie romp through the song with complete ease, both men having a thorough knowledge of harmony and a virtuosic technique on their instruments.

As mentioned, "Cherokee" ("Koko") is normally played in the key of Bb. Many bebop musicians, wanting to display their prowess, would call the song in F# or some other key that jazz players rarely use. Many a soloist left the bandstand with their tail between their legs!

Like "Koko," "Shaw 'Nuff" was another tune composed by Charlie Parker and trumpeter Dizzy Gillespie played over a pre-existing set of chord changes. This recording, which also dates from 1945 and is one of the earliest of all bebop records, again features Parker on alto sax and Gillespie on trumpet, this time backed by Al Haig on piano, bassist Curly Russell, and Sidney Catlett on drums.

This track demonstrates the magical way Gillespie and Parker played difficult and complex phrases together. These two inventors of bebop seem to breath as one. Their solos, too, are filled with aggressive, fiery lines played at an even faster tempo than "Koko."

"Shaw 'Nuff" is also uses AABA song form, with each section being 8 measures long. The chord progression comes from George Gershwin's popular Broadway song,"I've Got Rhythm." 'Rhythm Changes' as the progression became known, is still played often today. The A sections are made up of a 1-6-2-5 progression (from chords built on the first (1), sixth (6), second (2), and fifth (5) scale degrees). The bridge (B section) is a 2-5-1 progression for four measures in the key of the relative minor (a minor 3rd below the first scale degree; also can be thought of as a new minor key established on the sixth scale degree of the original major scale), which then moves for the last four measures to a 2-5-1 progression in the key of the dominant chord (a 2-5-1 progression in a new key established on the fifth scale degree of the original key). Complicated? You bet it is! If it were easy, anyone could do it.
