Gerry Mulligan

Next to Harry Carney, Duke Ellington's longtime baritone saxophone player, Gerry Mulligan was probably the best known and highly regarded baritone saxophone player in modern jazz. In addition to his remarkable abilities as a player, Mulligan was also a fine arranger, composer and bandleader who led a number of groups that helped to define the cool jazz movement.

Mulligan's first big break came when Gene Krupa hired him as both saxophonist and arranger in the 1940's. His bop influenced writing caught the attention of big band leader Claude Thornhill. It was in Thornhill's band that Mulligan met Gill Evans. This serendipitous meeting helped to lay the groundwork for Miles Davis' Birth of the Cool recording. Before the Birth of the Cool nonet played its engagement at the Royal Roost, Mulligan, Gil Evans, Miles and number of other musicians interested in something new were gathering at Evan's Manhattan apartment to play, arrange and refine the nonet's sound. Mulligan's contribution to the group and subsequent recording included arrangements of Darn That Dream and Godchild. He also composed and arranged three original songs Venus de Milo, Rocker and Jeru. Let's take a close look at Jeru.

Jeru incorporated a couple of devices that became standard cool jazz fare – shifting meters and phrases of unequal lengths. The tune basically in 4/4 meter and is an A-A-B-A form. The first two phrases (A-A) are the standard 8 bears long. Letter B, the bridge, is 12 bars long and has bars of 3/4, 2/4 and 6/4 in it. The last letter A of the form is 9 bars long rather than 8. Mulligan's use of mixed meters and phrases of varying lengths helped to create an arrangement full of unexpected twists and turns. Mulligan moved to Los Angeles after the Birth of the Cool recording sessions and helped to establish what became known as West Coast Jazz. The label is a misnomer as most of the people involved with West Coast Jazz were not from the west coast! Gerry Mulligan, for example, was born in Queens . New York – almost as far from the west coast as you can get and still be in the United States ! The label came to signify music played by white musicians in a very relaxed, laid back style totally ignoring the contributions being made by African-American musicians like tenor saxophonists Dexter Gordon and Buddy Collette who were performing in the LA area at the same time.

Before returning to New York in the late 1950's Mulligan and trumpeter Chet Baker formed a group with no piano in it. This piano-less quartet (sax, trumpet bass and drums) created quite a sensation and was recorded often by the recording company, Pacific Jazz. The lack of a piano left a lot space for the soloists to fill by themselves. They were now free to experiment with the chords and scales (harmony) and not be bound by the pianist. Because of this new found freedom, Mulligan and Baker were able to weave spontaneously created melody lines around each other. This counterpoint became a feature of cool jazz. Let's listen to Mulligan and Baker along with Carson Smith on bass and Larry Bunker on drums in a 1953 live recording of My Funny Valentine. An A-A-B-A form, the last letter A section is 12 bars long – 8 bars plus a 4 bar tag. The performance is two choruses long and is anchored by what seems to a written out (not improvised) bass line. The line is repeated with very few alterations for both the trumpet and baritone sax solos.
