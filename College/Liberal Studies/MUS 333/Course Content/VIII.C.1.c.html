New York Swing

“The Stampede”

“The Stampede,” recorded by the Henderson Orchestra in 1926, is a transitional work that contains elements of both Dixieland and Swing styles. The song was composed by Henderson and arranged by Redman. The rhythm section, with the inclusion of the tuba and banjo, is pure Dixieland. The tuba is playing on beats 1 and 3 of each bar throughout the entire performance, while the banjo accents beats 2 and 4. The resulting two-beat feel is standard for Dixieland. The horn background heard during the tenor saxophone solo is played on beat 2 of each bar, further establishing the two-beat feel.

Aside from the tenor solo background, the treatment of the horns is quite different from that of a Dixieland band horn section. The evolution toward Swing is clear in a number of ways.

  • There is no group improvisation. Only one instrument is featured in the solo sections.
  • The solos themselves are more lyrical. The improvised lines are much longer and are more melodic in character.
  • The horns not playing a solo are used strictly as accompaniment
  • There are distinct divisions between the band ensemble sections and the solos. An ensemble section ends and a solo section begins—a solo section ends and an ensemble section begins.
  • Both Don Redman as the arranger and his written arrangement have taken on greater importance. The arrangement is not improvised. Individual parts are provided for and are read by every player.

“The Stampede” is an ABAC form and features solos by trumpet players Rex Stuart and Joe Smith. Coleman Hawkins, one of the few players who made the transition from Dixieland through Swing to Bop, plays the tenor saxophone solo.

This wonderful arrangement and the fine playing point out the stylistic tug of war that was going on the time. The rhythm section was pulling back to Dixieland, while the arranged horns and individual solos were moving forward into Swing.
